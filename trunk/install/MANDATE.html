1. A Player’s View of the Game


Of the innumerable simulations in the world, most can be easily classified. There are wargames, there are diplomatic games, and there are games of civilization. And among these games there are obvious trade-offs. The wargame has the war already going while the civilization game treats war as an abstraction. Very few games have managed to merge the three types and still kept the flavour of each fully intact. Tribe Net blends the best and essential features of these three genres.

In Tribe Net a player operates his Tribe to the point of micro-management. He assigns people to feed the Tribe, make weapons, make tools, and scout the land. He has no information other than what he can gather himself. He is limited in his knowledge of terrain. He is limited in his knowledge of the reality of other Tribes. The fog of war can be truly of Pea Soup proportions. Decisions have far reaching consequences. Assign too few people to hunt or farm or fish and your people start starving. Fail to arm and they are vulnerable to bandits and outsiders. Fail to improve their ability to perform tasks and they remain primitive and inconsequential.

The Tribe’s abilities are categorised by certain tasks, make armour, make weapons, farm, hunt, herd, etc. And to allow these task abilities to be simulated they are graded on a scale of 0 to 10, with 0 being the most basic. The higher the skill the more difficult to attain it, and skills are obtained sequentially. You must have Farming-5 to go to Farming-6.

Tribes contain animals, goods, and people. All are precious and useful. You must have horses for your cavalry and your scouts; you must have elephants for transport. Cattle and goats are food on the hoof. You need traps and spears and bows for hunting. You need hoes or plows for farming. And all this stuff has to be made. Furthermore you have to locate the materials to make the stuff. The drive is always on to find minerals and coal, and to protect your supplies once you find them. And you must balance the work of your people. You have warriors, who can fight and work, Actives who can work. And Inactives (women and children) who do no work but are essential for reproduction. And all of them eat.

Operating a Tribe, getting it to grow and to become more powerful, may seem to be a worthy end in itself. But this is only the tip of the iceberg. Where Tribe Net makes its greatest deviation from the run-of-the-mill civilisation game is that the player determines what his Tribe is actually like, and interacts freely with the rest of the continent over E-mail. This is diplomacy in its purest form. Your knowledge of your colleagues is extremely limited, and the power that you can project can be real or illusory. You can chart your course as a lone wolf and speak to no one, or become a major player and be involved heavily in the discourse of the game. The diplomatic traffic is intense, and the impact is great. Through this a player can inject as much or as little colour into the game as he wishes. The only limitation being those that the player imposes on himself. There are evil Tribes and peaceful Tribes, slavers and freemen, religious blocs and xenophobic loners. Those Tribes who make the effort to speak up and involve themselves in the game find it rich and complex.

One factor that Tribe Net has that is almost totally absent anywhere else is Religion. In the days before nationalism (and indeed in many places in the world today) it is the religion by which the person identifies him or herself. Getting this to be realistically simulated can be almost impossible, but Tribe Net has managed to do it. Religions are invented by the players, and with the GM’s guidance the religion’s beliefs, and effects, are inventions of the players. The blocs that ideologies create in the modern world exist in Tribe Net with these religions, and whether you are a friend or an enemy can be determined solely by the religion of the Tribe. Religions can be warlike or peaceful, benign or cruel, and all the shaded areas in between. Mangalia currently has religions ranging from active cannibalism to fundamental anti-slavery.

When first entering the game a player will get a mentor. This is an experienced player who volunteered for the job and takes the time to work the player through the initial set-up and moves. The Mandate rulebook is freely available for players, but the Mentor is the one who makes clear the statements in the book and instructs the player how to translate the often bare descriptions into meaningful gameplay. The mentor can also instruct the player in what skills they should need right off since the mentor has learned that already, often the hard way.

The turn cycle is 1-month game time every 2 weeks. A player sends in his move, which details the activities of the Tribe and their skill attempts for that turn, and within a few days the gamemaster sends back the Return via E-mail. The return is the record of the consequences of that turns and its decisions, and the full status of the Tribe entering into the next turn with a full inventory of people, goods, and animals. The deadline for the turn is on a Tuesday, and the returns arrive on Saturday. The player then has a maximum of 1 week and 3 days to send in his move for the next turn. The cycle is as dependable as the sunrise in Tribe Net, and Peter is remarkably error free, especially considering the volume of information coming at him every week.

Most players of Tribe Net could easily live with a faster turnaround, but the 2-week period seems to work out best. Since diplomatic intercourse is fluid in Tribe Net the turn sequence and the player interactions rarely get disjointed. Players may have to have tighter communication if they are trading with each other or conducting joint manoeuvres in war, but this has proved easy to accomplish. The speed of E-mail can be truly amazing.

Players know the rules insofar as they know what they have to do, and what they have to have to accomplish something, but the mechanics are a mystery like the continent. A player will know to make bows, and know to make metal arrows, and know to have sufficient skill to use them correctly, but when he fires them at an enemy the only person who really knows what is going on to resolve combat is the gamemaster. Practice has shown this to be sufficient, though many players work hard to figure out the systems in order to better their chances in a given situation. Common sense most often reveals the same results as the best calculations.

The map that Tribe Net operates on is probably awesome. As the Tribes search out their surroundings it becomes quickly obvious that no matter how far they go there is more farther on. The theories on the shape of the continent are as varied and widespread as the theories on the shape of the world were in the actual year of 900 AD. Mapping is a precious commodity.

Tribes are identified by a unique number. This allows the gamemaster to keep track of the Tribe with no misdirections. Players, however, name their Tribe whatever they want. Some have changed the name of their Tribe more than once (prompting a validation of the GM’s use of numbers, they cannot change the number). Tribal names can be rich with meaning, or humorous. There are Tribes like the Hailong, the Chinese name of the Black Dragon. Yamato, a poetic term for Japan and a word embodying the Japanese Spirit. Kung Sah, the name of an infamous Malayan drug lord. The Oxwind, the Heck’r’we, the Sbaras, the GrossartigBastarde. Where a reference to “Tribe 449” inspires little, the same reference to “The Velvet Glove” tells you something about what you are facing.

But again, that is in the hands of the players.

Player interaction and effect is the hallmark of Tribe Net, and nowhere is that more apparent than in the research subjects. When a player achieves a 10 in a given skill that player may conduct research into something in that field. And what that is is up to the player. The player opens a dialogue with the GM where he proposes something, a new weapon or method of operating his people or his army, and the GM tells him what effect that will have once he is done. For example the player researching in Farming may research potatoes, which when planted are harvested in the fall and provide an efficient and readily utilised food source for the winter months when hunting is lean. The player with Herding 10 may research Pigs, which have litters rather than single or paired offspring and thus will reproduce like mad providing the herder with an inexhaustible source of food and leather. A new weapon, war-horses, war-dogs, Ninja, a player is limited only by his imagination. And the GM has shown himself to be very helpful and cooperative in working out research subjects.

A game with this many facets runs a high risk of being addictive, and it truly is. With its long time span and need for thorough planning and persistence it has tremendous appeal for the player who wishes to immerse himself in the game. But for the very casual player it will have little appeal. The game will return effort abundantly, but if little effort is made then the player simply drifts at the mercy of those players who do involve themselves. It can then be about as much fun as being a tennis ball. In order to get the most out of the game the player must involve himself in it, and the more involved the player is the more fun the game becomes. So if a person is a casual gamer, who likes beer-and-pretzel games that can be picked up and completed in a single hour or two then Tribe Net is not a good idea.

But if a player likes to immerse himself in a game and thinks that real-world simulations are for them, then Tribe Net would be a good investment.

  1. Mechanics


2.1 Turnaround Time

Turns (representing game months) are processed every two weeks (at Christmas there are two 3 week turnarounds and another 3 week turnaround around the middle of the year).


2.2 Basics

Tribe Net is a PBEM game in which players control Clans. Each Clan is made up of a number of Tribes which are mobile, and Villages which are stationary. Tribes/Villages can spawn Elements and Fleets. The general term applied to these entities is “Units”.


Clans are located on a hex(agon)-grid map aligned north-south, each hex represents 25 miles across. Thus directions are indicated by N, S, NE, NW, SE and SW. Your clan can move around the map by giving directional instructions during the movement turn. The setting is the Northern hemisphere.


Each turn you will receive a personal report on your results, any messages and a newsletter called Tribe Net.

Please note that although turns are database processed the game is NOT computer moderated. As a consequence there are bound to be occasional errors either of moderation or typing. Most errors are usually easy to fix but all players are urged to monitor their turns for possible mistakes and report them promptly (a simple note appended to the next turn will usually suffice.)


The GM is something like an umpire who tries to get it right, but is human and thus fallible. However you can be sure that the GM is concerned to preserve the integrity of the game while providing players with excitement and challenge. But since the game is open-ended the rules can never fully capture future developments and so some changes are bound to happen from time to time. When changes are made these will be communicated to the players as soon as possible.


2.3 Turn sequence

Game turns follow the following sequence (but not all steps may be needed).

Note that trade can happen before or after movement – but not during.


1. Miscellaneous orders (for example, for combat, Transfers, statements of truce, war)

2. Activities (each person/animal/item may be utilised for 1 activity per turn).

3. Tribe movement

4. Scout movement (trading while scouting)

5. Skill/research attempts

6. Provisions break-up (provs are calculated after the population increase).


2.4 Default turns

If no orders are received the default turn will put 70% of warriors to defence, the animals will be herded and remaining actives and warriors hunt (with no implements). The previous month's skills will be attempted. Population growth will occur. The Turn will incur normal costs.


2.4.1 Default orders

Default orders may be given to the GM. If the normal orders are not received the default orders will be used. These have to be clear or they will be ignored!

2.5 New Starts

In general I am happy for new players to start near their friends - as long as this does not compromise the position of other players who are in the general area, and if there is enough room. New starts may not attack nor be attacked for 10 turns. They may, however, engage in mutual Raiding.


2.5.1 Restarts

This option is available if the Clan loses 30% of People or 50% of Warriors (from the maximum numbers it enjoyed at any point in time).


1. If you have a single Tribe and opt for a restart then your new Clan will resemble that of a new player in all respects except that it is allowed 75% of the skill points your Tribe had on exit. No skills to be taken above 6, and no skill levels greater than those held by your Tribe at exit. And no skills that the old Tribe did not have. Religion must start at 0.

2. If you have Sub-Tribes then you may opt for the above (taking 75% of skill points of your Tribe with the most skill points - the Main Tribe).


Alternatively, you may take 60% of the Main Tribe's skill points plus 10% of the total skill points of your Sub-Tribes. No skill above 6 and no skill taken may exceed any "old" skill in level.


2.5.2 Taking over established Clans

Once players drop out so do their Clans - unless there is a specific request at the time for a new player to take over the position. This request must come from both the retiring and the incoming player. Any Credit or Debt will be retained. The GM reserves the right to make judgements on a case by case basis.

2.6 Late Orders

If Orders arrive late for 2 successive turns the Clan in question will suffer a loss of 40 Actives.

2.7 Other Documents

Along with your turn report you should expect to receive Tribe News. This is the turn newsletter that includes rule updates, player letters, addresses etc. There are two other documents that you will not receive in the startup distribution of materials. These are the Q&A document that contains a number of questions asked by the players over the years - it is quite chaotic in structure but quite useful if you use a specific word search routine. There is also the Combat document that provides some of the technical details of combat - weapon damage, armour effects, effect of terrain etc. However, I do not recommend that you ask for these until you are pretty comfortable with the rules and the game.

3. Basic Concepts


3.1 People

Each Tribe in your Clan is divided into 3 equal groups of people (a constant relationship) being:

Warriors: Who may perform EITHER a warrior activity or a general activity.

Actives: Who perform general activities.

Inactives: Who eat, breed & perform passive activities such as collecting water etc.

In addition there are other classes:

Slaves: Perform general activities. See Slavery/Overseer activity.

Ninja: Not much is known about these.

3.2 Morale

Each Tribe/village has a morale level that effects a number of factors in the game, combat being one of them.

Morale may be increased by:

  1. Winning battles (actually this is a guaranteed way to improve one’s mood!).

  2. Successful bidding at some Auctions.

  3. Eating 10% or more food than needed.

  4. Successfully increasing a Cultural or Religion skill.

  5. Successfully performing a Cultural activity with 1% of the Tribe’s population.

  6. Drinking alcohol, 10% of population receiving a beverage (1 barrel per 50 people), better quality alcohol would give a better chance of increase.

Morale may also be decreased - people dying is a good way to do this.

3.3 Provisions/water/fodder


3.3.1 Provs

Each turn you will need to supply 1 unit of provisions to each Tribe member and slave (dogs require a « unit). People not fed have a rebellious tendency to drop dead. Hunting supplies some provisions, any shortfall will need to be made up from your herds, fishing, baking, grain etc. Hunting occurs prior to eating each turn (see also Cooking).

Livestock

Goats = 4 provs Cattle = 20 provs Horses = 30 provs Elephants = 60 provs

People = 10 provs


Eating 10% extra Provs

Eat the requisite provs (that is, 10%) as an Activity under the Tribe that is attempting to raise Morale. Any overeating must be done by the Tribe (not Elements of the Tribe) – but the Tribe must eat enough to cover the number of people in the Element.


3.3.2 Starvation

Occurs mainly due to mismanagement of resources. Remember, even if you have a Unit in the same hex with sufficient provs to feed the starving group this will not save them (unless a Goods Tribe relationship exists). You must transfer the provs to the Unit in question (that is, provs are not automatically transferred to stave off starvation).


3.3.3 Water

Should the Tribe begin a turn in arid/desert/sea hex, or be under siege, water will be required.

People require 10 lbs of water. Slaves/Goats 5 lbs of water, Cattle / Horses require 20 lbs.

Water may be obtained automatically in any hex but arid/desert/sea. Empty vessels may be filled simply by stating so in your activities phase.


3.3.4 Fodder

Herd animals can't find enough to eat in snow hills/snow mountains/sea/desert. To offset starvation, fodder from your stores is needed (used automatically). Horses require 8 fodder, elephants 12, cattle 5, goats 1, sheep 1. Grain can be used in place of fodder (1 grain = 1 fodder). See also Villages under siege.

3.4 Tribal interactions

You can communicate, either directly or through the game with other Tribes. Such communications may provide you with useful maps/information (or lies).

3.5 Transfers/Trade

Transfers can occur Before Movement and/or After Movement (but not during Movement).

You should clearly indicate in your Orders if a Transfer is before or after Movement - if not, the trade will probably not be done.

To Trade items (skills and people may NOT be traded between players) there are three means available:


1. By sharing the same hex - that is, parties to the trade having a Tribe/element etc occupying the same hex at the same time.

2. By Scouting Transfer

You may designate one or more (of the eight) scouting group as a Scouting Transfer group. The Scouting Transfer group has 15 MV points if fully mounted and normal carrying capacity. It is possible to trade with more than one group using this method - and it is a two-way process. Scouts cannot use the Adjacent hex Transfer rule nor can they trade with other scouts directly - however, they can dump goods into a Storage Vault.

Storage Vault Transfers using Scouts

If you are transferring using a Storage Vault you should order “0500 to 0501 via Scouts to SV12000” where 12000 indicates the capacity of the Vault. The Vault must be capable of holding all goods transferred (and each of the two Scout groups involved must be capable of transferring the goods). Note: this effectively counts as a double transfer and the goods in question CANNOT also be transferred prior to Movement.

If any enemy unit with Suppressors is in the Storage Vault hex you can expect to lose a number of (if not all) goods.

3. By automatically transferring if the Tribes/Elements of the SAME CLAN are in adjacent hexes and at least one of them is a Village (that is, has a Meeting House). Note, this cannot be done in the turn the MH is built.



Note:

1. Any particular item may be transferred only once in a turn BM and/or once AM (for example, you cannot Order scouts to deliver 1 rope to 0460 which then passes it to 1460 using the adjacent hex rule - which then uses its scouts to ........................ etc). But you can transfer the same item BM then AM. In short, an item may be transferred a maximum of twice in any one turn.

2. Items transferred are not available for use until the next turn (the only exception is that provs can be eaten).

3. None of these options are available to Villages under siege.

4. It is possible to make your transfer of goods to another Tribe conditional upon receipt of items from them, but such conditions can only be interpreted strictly.

5. People can be transferred within a Clan on the same basis.

  1. If players do not indicate whether Transfers are before or after Movement they will not be done.

  2. Transfers via Ferry etc are an After Movement transfer.

  3. Transfers cannot be conditional upon the transfers Orders of other players.


Please try to make no more than ONE reference to any Transfers (for example, do not include them at the start of Orders and then again under an individual Tribe) - and try to include them all in one place in your Orders (at the start is best). And you do not need to make reference to receiving Goods.


3.5.1 Trade Routes

Trade Routes are possible with Economics research. They are DL5.

Each Trade Route needs to be researched separately.

A Trade Route must be specified as between two Villages (this will also determine the Movement point requirements between the two Villages).

A Trade Route can be a maximum of 120 MV points (but this can be added to with additional Research).

Once the Trade Route has been identified it can be used by any Tribe in the Clan (as long as the conditions below are satisfied).


A designated Element (which is part of the Tribe's Adm allowance) becomes the Trade Element. The Trade Element must contain at least 20 Warriors with metal weapons and armour.


The Trade Element may move 40 MV points in Fine weather, 30 in light/wind, and 20 in heavy (there is no distinction between mounted and foot Elements). Trade Elements move overland - but may cross rivers and lakes as long as Ferries and/or Barges are in place to service the Trade Element (that is, the craft are built and left in designated hexes). They may not travel through impassable terrain.


Movement of the Trade Element is shown as 30/90 (not as a normal Movement - that is, no terrain report etc). This means that the Trade Element has moved 30 points of the required 90 to reach the destination Village).

The Trade Element is "invisible" (but is based in Shanghai) and is not noted as present should it end a turn in the same hex as another Unit (locals are deemed to warn the Trade Element of any Attackers). However, Economics research (for example, Bribery) may enable the hijacking of a Trade Element or other subversive Activities that might be dreamed up by players.


A hostile Tribe on a Trade Route hex may block the Trade Element by announcing that they intend to do so - and by ending its Movement on the Trade Route hex (the GM will not accept conditional Orders like "if Trade Route crossed, stop and block"). However if a Trade Route is blocked goods are not lost and the Trade Element may return to the end of the Route which is not blocked.

Goods carried are limited by Carrying Capacity. Animals need to be Herded.

Movement allowance is doubled when travelling within National Borders (as long as the Trade Element is a member of the Nation).

Villages may be built on existing Trade Routes in order to take advantage of the Route.


3.5.2 Truces

Truces prevent Tribes from accidentally attacking each other (for example, a general attack order would not result in a battle). However the truce can be over-ridden by a specific order to attack a particular Tribe or to ignore truces. To declare a truce each player should state in the same turn that a truce is desired. The truce is effective from the start of the next turn. Truces may be broken by either party and the breach is effective from next turn.


3.5.3 War

A state of War may be declared against any non-truced Tribe. You will automatically attack these Tribes unless you specify in your turn that you won't. The war is effective in the turn following the declaration, you may retract it at any time - it is merely a standing attack order. To end a War both parties must agree in the same turn.


3.6 Splitting your Tribe

You can split off another Tribe or village from your Clan that will have its own separate skills and skill attempts. Or you can split off a fleet or mobile/immobile element. Elements/fleets continue to be a part of the Tribe/village and use the Tribe’s skills and don't develop their own. Note:Elements/Fleets must be smaller than the Tribe/village that spawned them.


3.6.1 Setting up a new Tribe/village

When you split off a village/Tribe from an existing Tribe/village the brand new creation will have no skills UNLESS you choose to give it some of the parent organisation’s skills. At no other time may skills be transferred.


A maximum of 3 Clans may occupy Villages in the same hex. A Village is here defined as either or both a fortified site and buildings.


3.6.2 Diplomacy and Tribe Splitting

You can't split off a Tribe/village unless you have a sufficient Diplomacy level.

The Tribes/villages etc are known collectively as “the Clan”. The highest Diplomacy level in the clan determines how many Tribes/villages you can break the clan up into.

You may have one Tribe or village per level of Diplomacy (in the most skilled Tribe or village) eg Dip 1 = 1 Tribe or village, Dip 2 = 2 Tribes or villages.


3.6.3 Transferring people

After you have started a new Tribe/village you may transfer people to it. But you may not transfer more than 5% + 1% per Diplomacy level above the level required for the number of Tribes/villages you have (or up to 90 people). This can occur in any turn. It is permissible to absorb the entire population of a Tribe or village into another, losing all the absorbed skills.

A Sub-Tribe can only be split from the Tribe (or one of its Elements) with the highest Diplomacy level. A Sub-Tribe can only be reabsorbed back into the Tribe with the highest Diplomacy level.

Up to 90 people may be transferred between any Units of a Clan regardless of Dip level. If this transfer is between sub-Tribes then the 1/3 ratio must be observed and retained (unless this is impossible to do). More than 90 people may be transferred if Dip levels allow it.


If exceptional circumstances arise which would violate the above conditions (for example, two Sub-Tribes being attacked or otherwise under threat, and where the circumstances would seem appropriate to justify an absorption of one Tribe into another) - the GM will make a judgement on a case by case basis.


3.6.4 Number of Units

A Tribe or village's permitted number of elements is determined by Admin level.

At Admin 1 you may have 1 element.

At Admin 3/5/7/9 you become entitled to an extra immobile Element/Fleet. You can an extra mobile elements at Adm5 and so on. So at Adm5 you could have 1 mobile and 2 immobile Elements or 2 mobile Elements.

Elements cannot be absorbed into a different Tribe from their parent Tribe where it would exceed the 5+% transfer rule 1.11.3.

3.7 The Meeting of the Clan

This MAY occur in any turn in which there are two or more Tribes/villages in the same hex. Note that this is ENTIRELY at the players discretion, it is NOT automatic;

1) Highest diplomacy determines the “senior Tribe” (if equal age of Tribe, size, leadership, then players choice)

This Tribe leads the clan assembled in war etc.

2) ONLY the senior Tribe may attempt Skill increases that turn (research is not affected).

3) People transfer follows normal rules.

4) All involved Tribes/Elements must stay together for the entire turn (that is, identical movement orders).

5) All a Tribe’s warriors assigned to combat help protect the entire clan - and will fight with the skill and morale of their particular Tribe.

6) May be called between Elements of the one Tribe, some, but not all, Sub-Tribes may participate, or indeed Elements of different Sub-Tribes (with or without parent participation) - however, in the latter case the parent Sub-Tribes have only one set of Skill attempts.

7) Must be announced at the start of the Turn.

8) Even if Tribes and their Sub-Tribes are in the same hex they may be attacked individually if not in Meeting of the Clan. If you suspect danger then Meeting of the Clan is not a bad option.


When in Meeting of the Clan all warriors are treated as if they are a single Clan (with weighted Com, Ldr etc applying to warriors – Healing is done by each sub unit).

Skill = (warriors in unit1*skill1 + warriors in unit2*skill2 + warriors in unitn*skilln)/(total warriors in Meeting of the Clan)

For example, if 200 warriors at Com6 combined with 100 at Com3 then the weighted skill would be (200*6 + 100*3)/300 = 1500/300 = Com5


However, the weighted Tactics level is rounded down to the next integer. If you ended up with say Tac3.1 then this would round down to Tac3.


3.8 Population balance & growth

If an imbalance in the active/inactive/warrior ratio occurs, eg by warriors or miners dying, or an element being split off, this imbalance will rectify over time. A balance of active/inactive/warrior is needed for population growth, with the least number being used as the basis for all three. That is, the population growth will be directed to the lowest of the three population figures until the proportions are equal.

Elements/fleets don't have to obey the active/inactive/warrior ratio. But to receive the benefit of breeding they would need a balance. If an element is re-absorbed the ratio will be restored immediately.

3.9 Hiring Ninja/Samurai (from other Players)

Ninja and Samurai may be hired to perform either Security type operations, or as an independent Element in the case of Ninja.

In order for a Clan to hire Ninja or Samurai you must gain Pol2 and Dip3 in the hiring Clan that may then supply the Ninja to any sub-unit of the Clan (the Tribe supplying the Ninja/Samurai does not need this).

Hiring requires an agreement between both players - this agreement is given to the GM and serves as a formal Contract. Once the GM has ratified the Contract, funds are transferred (either Silver or Gold).

Once the Contract is in place the hired Ninja/Samurai is/are effectively under the control of, and placed with, the hiring Tribe for the length of time specified in the Contract (which must be in units of 12 months).

Security

An Element from the hiring Tribe is located in Shanghai (the Tribe supplying the Ninja DOES not need to do this). This Element is free (of game charges) but counts as an immobile Element against Adm levels. (The Element is automatically placed in Shanghai for the duration of the hire period - which is 12 months and is essentially a “paper” Element). This works like the Ninja hired via Auction.

Hired Ninja/Samurai under Security may only act as a Security Force (Samurai may not fight for the hiring Clan - though research could allow this possibility).

Element

An Element from the hiring Tribe is located in Shanghai (as above). The Element must be composed entirely of Ninja (hired from the same Clan). The player hiring the Ninja gives it its Movement and other Orders. To do this though, the hiring Tribe must create a separate mobile Element which incurs game costs. Once this is done the Ninja is automatically located in the Element of the hiring Clan.

Conditions

1. The hiring Tribe may not kill the Ninja/Samurai - this principle is deeply embedded in Mangalian Codes of Honour. The hired Ninja/Samurai may conduct no hostile activities (like Spying) vs its host.

2. The two Clans involved in the Contract may not belong to the same Religion.

3. Once the contract time elapses a renewal may be negotiated or else the hired force reports overland to its Tribe of origin.

3.10 Goods Tribe

One Tribe can be the holder of the goods and animals for other designated Tribes of the same clan, any goods needed or produced will be recorded with the Goods Tribe.

While the idea of the GT works quite well, it needs to be fully understood and requires vigilance. Here are some comments:

1.The GT acts as a storehouse for all sub-units (SU’s) using it - and thus a GT may provide goods for the Fair or other Trade..

2.Any Transfers from a GT to its SU’s and vice versa will not work - and are not necessary.

3.The GT should be used only when positions are relatively settled and should not be changed often (and certainly not in mid-Orders).

4.You should (and in fact, must) make reference to the GT in your Orders only if there is a change of status (for example, if a particular Tribe is no longer acting as the GT) or if the correct status has not appeared on your Report. If changing GT status please use, for example, “Change GT to xxxx before Activities”.

5.Announcing that a SU is to use a GT will not of itself transfer the SU’s existing goods into the GT. If this is desired then it should be done prior to the announcement of the GT.

Note: the GT will not operate into adjacent hexes.

5.8.5 Blockading a Ford/Pass

A Ford/Pass may be blockaded by either a Tribe in the Field or behind Fortifications. This will occur on the turn after the Unit takes up a blockading position.

Units positioned on a Ford or Pass may be ordered to observe passage of other Units (but not Scouts) - the chances of success are high but not guaranteed. This may be ordered on the turn after the Unit takes up position.

Note: blockading a Pass is only effective if the Tribe attempting passage through is dependent on the Pass for its Movement. For example, if it is hauling wagons with insufficient elephants. Should anyone attempt to block a Pass through Low Snow Mnts this would be highly effective.


Field: the Defender is deemed to be defending from the Riverbank as the Attacking troops wade across. If the Defending Tribe(s) are defeated in Combat then any Tribes trying to cross the Ford in order to enter or move through the Defender’s hex may do so. The Combat is treated as a 5 phase single sector battle.

In the case of a Ford, the Defender’s hex is on the other side of the River (from the Attacker). In the case of a Pass the Defender’s hex is in the Mnts that the Pass leads into (that is, the Defender is denying access into the Pass).

Fortifications: a Fortified site can defend a Ford/Pass in the manner above as long as the Defender opts to take the Field. If the Defender does not opt to take the Field it allows attacking Tribes access to its hex - but not passage through the hex. Passage through the hex by the Attacking Tribes is possible if: (a) the Defender is defeated, (b) the Defender is placed under Siege. If two or more Tribes are attempting passage through the hex they may do so on the turn following the defender being placed under Siege (as long as the Siege is retained).

If multiple Tribes are defending then similar rulings will apply (for example, they must all be defeated in the Field before passage across a Ford is possible).


Scouts/Ninja: Scouts attempting to move through Fords/Passes to Locate etc are much more likely to be spotted by any Suppressors. Ninja are unaffected (since they do not need Fords to cross Rivers).

8. TRIBE (GENERAL) ACTIVITIES


Every turn you can perform tasks with your Tribes, Elements and Villages. General Activities are performable by all these units.

There are three basic types of skills. Those where skill level affects what can be produced, those where level affects the productivity of each person assigned and those where level determine how many people can be assigned. In the latter case, where you see “10 people per skill level” this refers to the sum total of people working in the Tribe and any of its Elements. For example, a Tribe with For1 is entitled to 10 foresters total (not 10 foresters in the Tribe and 10 in an Element).

When creating your orders please give the skill used, what is being attempted and the number of people involved, and whether any special items are being used. Warriors and active’s should not be separated.


For example:

Hunting Provs 1000 100 bows, 200 slings, 300 traps

Mining Coal 645 (400 people+245 slaves), 500 picks

Metalwork Picks 50 bronze


Note that goods produced in Activities (and Buildings built) are only available on the following turn.

8.30 Slavery

8.30.1 Slaves

Slaves work as Actives, but can be transferred between Tribes with no limit.

Slaves must be overseen by Warriors or Actives or they may attempt to flee.

Slaves will work for the Tribe holding them, they can be passed to another Tribe like goods and will work for that Tribe at the skill level of the new Tribe.

A Tribe (or sub-Unit) may hold no more than 1 slave per Clan member present (2 if shackles are used)*.


* Fleets may carry 10 Slaves for every Warrior that is overseeing them (at Slv0). In Naval combat there are similar restrictions to normal combat on Slaves that may be captured in battle - however, overall Slaves held by a Fleet is limited only by overseers available, Slv levels, and accommodation on vessels. Note this rule applies only if Fleets are actively engaged on the water. A Fleet that is based on land is treated as an ordinary Unit for the purposes of slaves that may be held.


8.30.2 Overseeing

At Slavery 0 you must assign 1 Warrior as overseer per 10 slaves

At Slavery 1 you may control 1 slave per 10 people present without an overseer

At Slavery 2 you control 2 slaves per 10 people - at level 10 you control 10 per 10 people.

Shackled slaves count as half each for control and overseeing purposes.


Note: Slaves in the Goods Tribe can only be used by the GT.  They are not available to any Sub-units.  They must be moved to the unit that needs them. Slaves may be transferred within a Clan at the start of Orders.


8.30.3 Overwork

Slaves can be overworked, they need a overseer with a whip per 10 slaves (see leatherwork).

For each slavery level the slaves will be 10% more productive.

eg. At slavery 3 100 slaves will do the work of 130 active’s.

The slave population will decrease at about 10% due to deaths from overworking. (Their way of protesting).

Warriors, horses and equipment not used for other activities are available for the Tribe’s defence (or offence) against bandits and hostile Tribes.

You do not have to state how many warriors are on defence, if a battle is to take place the GM will notify you of the number of warriors left after all activities are performed. However those assigned to security, suppression and scouting should have their numbers and equipment stated.


9.2 Combat and Movement

Combat may occur at any time in the turn, and may occur more than once. For example, if you start the turn in a hex with another Tribe you may attack them (which is deemed to happen before Activities in the turn), move off, and attack someone else. But Combat cannot occur during Movement. That is, Units in combat must be in the same hex at the start of Movement or at the end of Movement.

9.3 Scouting

(see also Movement/Weights)

Warriors may be assigned to scouting parties. Scouts go out from the Tribe after the Tribe has moved. You may use up to 8 scouting parties per Tribe/Village (element’s scouts count against its Tribe’s total), keeping in mind:-

  1. Warriors/horses/items assigned to scouting parties may not be used for other activities that turn.

Scouting is dangerous, there is no guarantee that scouts will return, especially scouting skill is low.

As well as mapping out territory, scouts may perform one mission in their final hex, unless stated otherwise all scouts will search, if assigned another mission which cannot be performed then search will become the scouts’ mission.

9.4 Scouting missions

Note that these specific missions will greatly reduce the chance of your scouts having any sort of find, but if you desire a greater chance of the specific mission at the expense of overall discoveries then this option exists.

9.4.3 Spy

If a group of people is encountered they will be spied upon. Spy skill is useful to do this safely (See Security and Spy skill)

For every level of Spying you may nominate to find an exact amount of any particular item held by the spied upon Tribe. A Clan's Skills are not within the ambit of Spying.

Similar to spy but tries to relieve other clans of possessions, you may like to specify a type of item you would like (eg weapons/horses/people for slaves). A large scout group raiding may precipitate a full battle.

Scouts may locate Tribes in any hex they pass through - if successful they will lead a war party back to the victim’s hex. (The war party is taken from the Tribe’s defence warriors). See below. If in Meeting of the Clan, the total number of defenders is counted as the War Party.

9.5 Security

Warriors assigned to Security will attempt to discover the presence of scouts from other clans. They will also attempt to discover the presence of spies and raiders - the skill of Security will help with this. Spies and raiders will be attacked, but normal scouting will only be noted. 50% of the Warriors assigned to Security will be able to Defend should the need arise (Suppressors will not be available). Specification of the cavalry component of your Security force could be advantageous. Note that Suppression (see below) requires its own Warriors assigned in addition to any on Security.


9.6 Suppression and Locating


Suppression

Suppression is an extroverted form of Security. Warriors assigned will attempt to intercept scouting parties entering the Tribe’s hex, they attack scouts seen except their own and those of Truced Tribes (and they may specify not to attack named, read numbered, units). Suppressors are the only defence against scouts attempting to Locate - but Suppressors will, if successful, suppress all scouts from the one Tribe using Locate Orders (depending on numbers, equipment and skills, some scouts from the Locating party might nevertheless escape to report their findings).

In the ordinary course of things a successful Suppression will not learn the specific orders of the scouting group - but in some cases they may (a hidden % roll is in play here).

Warriors assigned to Suppression will not be able to participate in defence. Suppression is a separate activity to defence. If the suppression force is not strong enough to deal with the locating scouts (or fails to detect them) then the locating may still take place.


There are two skills relevant to Suppression - they are Scouting and Security. Other factors in play are terrain, numbers of scouts, weapons and whether and numbers mounted. These may or may not conspire against your endeavours.


Tribes with Suppression Orders will not suppress the scouts of Truced clans unless:

1.The player announces that they wish to do so,

2.The truced clan in question is using Locate Orders (that is, if scouts with Locate Orders move into a hex with a Truced Tribe with Suppression Orders, those orders will be active vs ANY scouts using Locate).


Multiple Units

If there are multiple Units (from the same or different Clans) using suppression in a hex then Scouts entering the hex negotiate Suppressors from two units only. These are selected randomly (though if a randomly selected unit has zero assigned to suppression it does not count towards the limit). If the scouts are not detected (and/or fully dealt with) by the first group of suppressors, the second group comes into play. For example, Raiders vs AAAA might have to deal with Suppressors from BBBB, then from AAAA before they get to Raid AAAA. Similarly, Locating scouts might need to evade or survive vs Suppressors from AAAA before evading or surviving Suppressors from BBBB (any losses vs AAAA will be deducted prior to resolution vs BBBB).


Locating

Locating is deemed to occur in any hex passed through (not just in the final hex) - though your Scouts will return to report the first target they Locate (that is, they will not continue into the next hex). However, unless instructed otherwise they may Locate more than one target in the first hex.

Locate is an aggressive Order that indicates an intent to attack. Some clans have and are using this order in its intended sense, others are using it as an innocent attempt to simply find things (if you wish to do the latter order "Patrol" or "Search").


There are two skills relevant to Locating - they are Scouting and Spying - if you are very low in one or the other you can expect the Suppressor to have a chance of discovering your mission (and if they do, it is highly unlikely that you will know they have).


Should you be lucky enough to Locate more than one target your Warriors will be committed to more than one combat. Should this not be desirable you should say, for example, "Locate one target only". You cannot successfully Order "Locate target with fewest defenders" unless you have a successful Spying mission into the same hex with instructions to make this judgement (though such a Spying party can work in hexes passed through).

A Locate Order will override a Truce (so you should say "Locate only untruced Tribes" - if this is what you desire).


If you are Locating you would probably want your Tribe with Standing Attack Orders (like "Attack any Tribe/Element" - or "Attack any unTruced Tribe" - which will happen should you occupy the same hex during or at the end of Movement).


A Locate commits you to a battle, but if neither party wants to fight then they should both indicate “Defend” in their Combat Orders.


9.7 Torture

This activity, aided by its own skill is performed (by a Warrior) to enhance the memory of an absent minded captive.

Torture must be performed immediately after Slave capture (rather than waiting for the following turn). That is, it will be conducted and resolved in the week following the Turn Report. Questions should be listed in the order asked.

The chances of successfully having a question answered = (10% + TorLvlx10)%. One question per discrete group captured (for example, capturing 2 Scouting groups would allow 2 different questions to be asked). One question may be put to up to 5 clients using 1 torturer (thus increasing the chances of an answer). Each client that fails to provide an answer, dies in the process (not unlike our present education system!). Questions should relate to the Clan in question (and not to what other Clans are doing etc).

Any people employed as torturers mid turn may not be used the following turn.




10. COMBAT “The Battlefield”



Incredible as it may sound, there are some anti-social xenophobes, lurking round the hexes, just waiting to assault your Tribe.

There are also bandits, brigands and other ruffians ready to take advantage of an undefended or poorly defended Tribe.

Believe it or not, there are some wimps out there who assign fewer warriors to defence than you do....just ask your spies....or theirs.

10.1 Initiating combat

1) You may encounter a Tribe which attacks you.

2) You may be in the same hex as a Tribe (or its element) with whom you are at WAR.

3) You might order an attack yourself - there are several ways to do this:

a) Order an attack on a specific Tribe(s) (including truced Tribes)

b) Order an attack on any Tribe (truced Tribes are presumed to be exempted unless specified otherwise)

c) Order an attack as above BUT with the attack conditional upon weather/terrain etc.

d) You may attempt to ambush a Tribe (see Tactics)

e) Note that you can attack Tribes which have been located by your scouts - see scouting activity. Scouting skill will determine if your warriors are successfully led to the prospective defenders.

10.2 Availability for combat

Warriors are only available for combat if they performed NO other activity that turn. The same applies to equipment and horses. If there is pre-Activity combat the goods held at the end of the previous turn are those available (note though that losses due to such combat will not be known to you until after your turn, and thus Activities, has been processed, in effect then you are allowed to assign people to Activities who might actually be dead – in some places they call this the Public Service).

Your Tribes and Villages expect to be defended (although walled Villages expect less in terms of proportion of warriors available for defence according to level of defenses). Failure to maintain a reasonable sized defence force can have deleterious effects upon morale (to say nothing of your future in the game).


Note: if you have sub-Tribes occupying the one hex (without ditch/walls etc) they must be defended individually. That is, an aggressor moving into the hex gets to fight ALL sub-Tribes. If some of these have Zero defenders then they are wiped! Note also, if you have built a big ditch without water supply a sieging force will quickly bring you to your knees.


10.3 Routing

A badly outnumbered force is likely to rout, abandoning equipment. Low morale and losses make routing more likely. If your forces rout the enemy may get to pillage your camp, as well as gaining the battlefield spoils.

A unit that fully routs (that is, loses the battle) vs multiple opponents, will not be available to fight against further opponents in that turn.


10.4 Combat orders

If you become involved in a combat you will be informed and will be requested to submit “combat orders” with your next moves. You will be told who you are fighting, what the terrain and weather is like, how many warriors and what equipment are available.

Warriors and equipment may be assigned to the following groups:-

Each group should be made up of troops equipped in the same fashion

Melee troops :- Cavalry

Infantry

Missile troops :- Archers (inc slingers)

Heavy weapons (Ballista’s. Note Catapults are too long distance for use in field combat. Each ballista requires a crew of 4.)


Another decision which must be made is whether you wish to attack or defend.

This must be stated for each of the three sectors.

If you attack your forces will charge at the enemy.

If you defend, your infantry will hold steady with any cavalry vs the first wave of attackers (presumably their cavalry).

Please include the number of Troops fighting with no weapons.

Insert your figures directly into the example Table provided. Please insert your skills into the other Table. If you are in Meeting of the Clan the skills (except for Healing) should be weighted.


Note: if Combat Orders are not received within the due period (60 hours after distribution of Combat Requests) then Warriors assigned to defense are assumed to Defend. The GM will distribute any equipment available. If there are multiple players/units involved, any units that would ordinarily have been overrun will be overrun (for example, two units at War with each other where one has assigned sufficient defenders and the other one has assigned zero will still result in an overrun even if Combat Orders are not received by the due time).

If both sides Defend there is no detailed Combat Report.


Multiple Battles

Where there are multiple battles (for example, A and B vs C and D) occurring in a hex the order is determined randomly by the GM. A possible order might be A vs D, and B vs C (simultaneously) then A vs C and B vs D (simultaneously). This is the case in Field AND Siege/Assault attacks.

However, where the multiple battle consists of A and B vs C (only) in a Siege/Assault then A and B may indicate the order in which they wish to attack. If the defender (in this case C) chooses to enter the Field then the order of attack becomes random. The typical scenario is a planned Siege/Assault against a single site. Note that A and B may suggest an order of attack in situations where there are two or more sites in a hex as long as they are attacking only one. If a second site decides to enter considerations then the GM may have to make a judgement regarding the order.


In short, the GM reserves the right to determine the order of attacks if there are not clear factors in play.



10.4.1 Conditional Orders in Combat

Conditional Orders should refer not to what the opposition is doing as to what you are doing - otherwise a vicious circle may develop. For example, Player A Orders “send the Reserve to the Flank where Player B does not send their Reserve”. Player B Orders “send the Reserve to the Flank where Player A sends their Reserve” (or something like this - formal logic was not my strong suit!).

So, for example, in an Assault an Order like “set up camp and the Reserve in the enemies weakest Sector”, or in the Field, “place all Cavalry opposite the enemies Infantry” are not acceptable.


You can conditionally deploy troops according to your own numbers and loses, nor according to your opponents (perceived!?) weaknesses, strengths etc, etc. This includes making your attack conditional upon the number of troops opposing you. You are given overall numbers of Troops on each side when the Battle Requests are sent. It is up to your Spies, and other Intelligence, and your intuitions to determine whether you should attack or not. Sometimes you will attack against unanticipated strengths – this simulates the “Fog of War”.


10.4.2 Running Away

Tempting though it is to request, as part of Movement Orders, “if outnumbered, run away”, some consideration must be given to the possibility that this becomes a standard and universal strategy. Mangalia would become too friendly even for a pacifist like myself. In short, this order does not work. If you go looking, expect to fight!


10.4.3 Quitting during the Fight

Should a player choose to drop out during a Combat and deny their opponent a rightful victory and spoils, the GM shall make a judgement - which might amount to something like 10% of the vanquished army joining the “Victors”.

10.5 Skirmishing

When two opposing forces encounter and both are under 100 or so in number the battle is regarded as a skirmish.

Unlike major battles, skirmishes only have one battle line and not three sectors. The combat will be run by the GM who will make the appropriate attack and defence decisions for the Tribes in question.

Orders can be placed with your elements for particular circumstances, eg, “Run away if outnumbered”.

10.6 Overruns

If one side has a clear numerical advantage (all things being equal this is a 10:1 advantage), then the defenders are overrun. However, the battle will again be “played out” by the GM to make sure the defending force doesn’t get away by a fluke. Otherwise the player will be notified of the loss of his brave warriors. Fortifications will effect the outcome for the defenders.

If a position is fortified it cannot be overrun as such. It will surrender however if it is clear that the position will take 40% casualties in the attack (that is, in some cases the battle will not be played out - but the attacker needs sufficient Siege Equipment and/or missile weapons to do this). Should this be the case 40% of the occupants are deemed to be killed prior to Slave taking.


An overrun results in a Surrender - no casualties occur to either side. All goods held by the overrun Unit are lost. Slaves taken by the attacker may be no greater than the number of attacking Warriors/2 (plus adjustments for Shackles). The attacking Tribe/Clan may hold no more slaves than permitted by its Slavery limits. Should Slaves taken be less than the overrun Units population, the Unit will survive. No TP’s are gained though Morale is 0.02 up for the winner and 0.02 down for the loser.


Should the attacker decide not to, or is otherwise incapable of taking Slaves, then people may be killed at the “Slave taking rate” - for example, if an attacker is entitled to take 100 Slaves it may opt instead to kill 100 people.


10.7 The Battlefield

The standard battlefield is divided into 3 sectors, the centre, left flank and right flank.

The disposition of your Warriors is limited according to your TACTICS level.

The basic requirement is that 50% of Warriors must be allocated to the centre with 25% each to the flanks. Within this limitation Missile troops may be allocated in whatever manner you desire.

That is, the overall numbers of Warriors in a Sector must satisfy the 25/50/25% requirement but within any Sector the ratio of Archers to Melee/Cavalry etc is up to the players discretion.


At Tactics 2 you may allocate between 20-30% of Warriors to one flank. The other flank must still have 25%.

At Tactics 6 you may attack in oblique(not straight line) formation; one flank may have 25-50% of Warriors, the other must have 25%, the centre has the remaining 25-50%.


10.8 The Reserve

You may have 10% of your Warriors per tactics level assigned to the reserve.

Troops from the reserve may be released to a sector depending upon conditions specified by the player. Troops from the reserve may only be released in a manner which keeps total troop numbers committed to within 5% of tactical limits (based on the total number of melee troops - including those in the reserve even if they are never committed to battle). Reservists can't take part in the battle before the last two phases.


The Troops in the reserve in the Field may do one of two things:


1. Defend the camp from looting in the case where one or more of the battlefield sectors is routed.

If one or more battlefield sectors are routed the entire reserve will automatically engage any enemy troops that are headed toward the camp. Even if outnumbered the reserve could at least hope to slow down the attackers thus minimizing the camp loot, also, with all else being equal, reservists would be somewhat less likely to rout than battlefield troops.


2. The entire reserve may be sent to bolster one sector if either the sector is shaken or it suffers losses of a number specified by the player.


If there is more than one condition for releasing the reserve the player must put them in order of priority.

For example, "Reserve to defend camp, however, if no sector has routed by the end of phase 3 then join the sector that has taken greatest percentage losses".


10.9 Combat resolution

Combat is resolved in the following phases

1) Missile phase (encompassing the effect of missiles over the course of the battle (if both sides defend then this is the end of the battle).

2) Cavalry melee

3) General melee 1

4) General melee 2


In phase 2, the opposing cavalries encounter (if one side lacks cavalry then the cavalry encounter infantry in this phase).

You may have both infantry & cavalry in a sector, however if your cavalry numbers are few you may wish to delay the cavalry charge by 1 round to coincide with the infantry. To do this, order them to hold in phase 2 and attack in phase 3 with the infantry given a normal attack order.


If defenders are running short during a melee phase the attacking forces will happily carve up missile troops.


10.10 Combat Rules

A separte document contains more details regarding the mechanics of Combat. Should players want to see this they should let the GM know.


10.11 Factors influencing combat

Are terrain, weather, combat skill, archery skill, heavy weapons, leadership skill, terrain proficiency, weapons and armour used and morale.

Cavalry will have a skill equal to the lower of combat & horsemanship.

10.12 Assigning equipment

Cavalry/infantry should be assigned a weapon, they may also be assigned a shield, helm, chain mail AND breastplate (or an equivalent).

Missile troops may also be equipped with a melee weapon and armour, but they cannot employ shields.

10.13 Your leader

It is presumed that your leader is directing the battle from a fairly safe position. However, you may choose to have your leader take a prominent and dangerous part in the battle to inspire your troops to greater efforts. Your leader will have the most effect in the sector to which they are assigned, lesser in adjacent sectors. If your leader is wounded all advantage is lost and there is chance that your troops will rout in panic.

10.14 After the battle

If one (or more) enemy sector has routed, you get to loot the battlefield. You get to loot the enemy camp if all three enemy sectors rout (in addition to looting the routed sectors).

All wounded warriors will be tended by the tribal healers. Good healing skills, herbs (1 herb treats 1 warrior), reasonable weather and terrain and not fleeing the battlefield all improve the chances of a warrior recovering.

10.15 Brucegeld

If you have an attack order and are not attempting ambush you may offer “brucegeld”.

The brucegeld is an amount (measured in silver) that will “buy off” your attack - it should also come with a list of commodities you will accept for payment and their value in silver for this purpose.

If brucegeld is offered both players MUST still submit combat orders.


10.15.1 The attacker has several options

1) He can choose to accept brucegeld if the defender pays it and call off attack.

2) He may choose to reject the brucegeld.

3) He may secretly lower the brucegeld in anticipation that the defender will make a counter-offer.

4) He may decide not to attack even if brucegeld isn't paid.


10.15.2 The defender can

1) Ignore the offer and prepare to fight.

2) Attack the offerer, enforcing a battle (so they can't pull out).

3) Accept the offer - payment is only made if the attacker doesn't reject payment.

4) Make an offer of a lower amount and hope that the attacker will lower their demands.


10.15.3 Fiefdom


Fiefdom is a Brucegeld option for players (rather than a rule).

1. If a Clan/Tribe is in danger of being knocked out of the game it may offer to subjugate itself to its attacker. That is, to offer itself as a Fiefdom. The attacker, (hereafter known as the Lord), may or may not accept. If accepted the whole Clan has Fiefdom status.

2. A Clan in Fiefdom may carry on all activities, alliances, religious worship etc as it had - however, it may not attack its Lord (note that this does not mean that the Fief will not transfer weapons etc to the Lord’s enemies – unless this is written into the agreement between Lord and Fief).

3. The terms of the Fiefdom are to be negotiated between the players - but a minimal idea is that the Fief would automatically transfer a quantity of goods to the Lord once per year (regardless of location). Once the agreement is in place it cannot be broken unless the Fief is liberated.

4. Liberation occurs if the Lord is forced to give up the Fiefdom (the Lord being knocked out of the game is the most obvious way in which this would come about). The Fief holder always has the option of granting freedom.

5. Any Clan under Fiefdom may not participate in the benefits of Nation or Statehood.


10.16 Tactics

Apart from battlefield options, your tactics skill helps determine your success in ambushing another Tribe.

Relative encumbrance, terrain, weather, as well as tactics, scouting, leadership skills will be important.

If successful (and this is not determined until it is time to resolve the combat) the victim loses their missile fire, cavalry charge, and are at greatly reduced efficiency in the first phase of melee. An unsuccessful attempt at ambush has the reverse effect.

If ambushing you cannot offer brucegeld.


10.17 Ambush


Orders to Ambush should be placed in Miscellaneous Orders. An ambush works best when the ambushing Tribe is stationary and the victim has moved in the turn. Obviously factors like Terrain, Weather, skill Levels come into play.

If the ambush is successful the victim loses their missile fire, cavalry charge, and are at greatly reduced efficiency in the first phase of melee. An unsuccessful attempt at ambush has the reverse effect. If you are under Tac6 and/or your opponent has higher Tactics than you, you should think very carefully about trying this strategy. However “Ambush” would make a lovely research topic. Units using fortifications may not engage in Ambush.

You cannot give conditional orders to attack only if the ambush is successful - these would be in contradiction with the idea that you have been sprung.


10.18 Special circumstances

Combats while crossing fords and landing from ships allows the defender extra benefit during the missile phase (while the attacker is struggling ashore) and the first melee phase (while the bridgehead is expanded). Cavalry cannot charge across a ford or up a beach.


11. Combat - “Sieging a Village”


A Village may be attacked in two ways:

  1. Assault: results in combat only.

  2. Siege: cuts of the Village from the outside world and traps the defenders within the Village walls.

On the first turn (only) of a Siege, or any turn of an Assault, the defender may decide to abandon the site and pursue field combat instead.

To attack a Village you need to give the general order “Siege” or “Assault”, you may not keep moving after siege combat.

Note that besieging and defending forces are liable to suffer from disease and sanitation skill will reduce the chance of this occurring during a protracted siege.

To siege a Village you will need to maintain in each of the 6 sectors 2 melee troops per yard of perimeter.

If at ANY stage the number of troops in any sector falls below this number, the siege is broken.

11.1 Tactics and initial disposition

Assault Warriors must be deployed (initially) in equal numbers in all Sectors, as do Siege Troops (with Tactics/Reservist adjustments). However, within any Sector the numbers of Archers, Infantry and Pioneers can vary (as long as the overall numbers of Assault Troops are equal in number across Sectors (noting Tactics).


Siege: each sector must have a minimum of (16 minus tactics level)% melee troops initially assigned.

Assault: each sector must have a minimum of (16-2 x tactics level)% melee troops initially assigned.


A Village under Assault (or Siege) may not conduct Activities such as Hunting and Mining if DeVA is announced by the attacker - though it may conduct Village Activities - including Fishing if it has a waterfront without unfriendly vessels. If under Siege or DeVA a Village may not Scout, or Transfer Goods to outside Units.


11.1.1 Waterfront Sites


If a Village site has water fronting one of its Sectors (as any Village on a River, Lake or Ocean would) then a Siege against it is not possible unless the sieging force can also put vessels into the water (to satisfy the numerical requirements for a siege - that is, 2 people per yard - if the Village also has ships in the water). Note: a Village may have only one waterfront sector.

However, an Assault may be carried out against the 5 "land fronted" sectors - where each sector must have a minimum of (16-2 x tactics level)% melee troops assigned by the Attacker.

Neither the Attacker nor Defender need assign troops to the waterfront sector during an Assault (though the Defender might give conditional Orders to Reservists in case the Attacker has vessels). The defender needs to assign (Defenders minus Reservists)/5 warriors to each Sector (as either Infantry or Archers).

The Defender needs to assign (Defenders minus Reservists minus Sortiers)/5 warriors to each Sector (as either Infantry or Archers) if this is a waterfront or (Defenders minus Reservists minus Sortiers)/6 warriors to each Sector if there is not.



11.2 Combat phases

Sieges operate in 5 phases, for each sector you have the choice in each phase of whether it is to be a

preparatory (Prep) phase or an assault.

Prep phases let your pioneers work, your archers and ballista’s to fire at enemy troops and catapults to fire at the walls. Assault phases have only your infantry attacking - however Archers may continue to attack if attacking Infantry are attacking through a breach (for example, they will not fire if Siege Towers are being deployed).


11.3 Village vs Archers

Should a Village site with ditch or moat (that is, no walls) be assaulted by archery fire only (that is, no melee troops cross the ditch/moat) it is possible that the defender might lose the Battle but lose no goods (unless animals are left outside the site).

However, should the assaulting archers cause one or more sectors to rout they are able to fire on the routing warriors and (1/5th or 1/6th) of the Village Actives and Inactives in the sector - with no armour. Under these circumstances it is possible that the Village might lose enough population to capitulate completely in the turn of assault. That is, they might surrender without any Infantry having set foot inside.

If you fear that this could happen to you there is the option of engaging the attacker in the field on the first turn of the Assault - where the consequences of rout are nasty but not as dire.

Assault troops (which are Infantry, Archers and Pioneers) are within Village archery range, Siege Troops are not (but are exposed to HvyW). Troops assigned to the Reserve during an Assault are also deemed to be outside Village missile range.

Assault troops must be deployed (initially) in equal numbers in all Sectors, as do Siege Troops (with Tactics/Reservist adjustments). However, within any Sector the numbers of Archers, Infantry and Pioneers can vary (as long as the overall numbers of Assault Troops are equal in number across Sectors (noting Tactics).

Assault phases have only your infantry attacking - however Archers may continue to fire if attacking Infantry are attacking through a breach(es). Heavy Weapons cannot be used vs Sectors in which troops are moving across or through breaches.

11.4 Camp and reserve

The attacker must specify the Village sector which their camp is adjacent to. This is the area in which the reserve will be held. The reserve will be committed according to the attackers instructions. Uncommitted reserve troops will defend the camp from any Village troops breaking through.

10% of troops per tactics level may be assigned to the reserve.

The reserve starts at camp (adjacent to a designated sector) and is one phase away from that sector's troops – thus the Reserve may join the existing troops in this sector in phase2. However, at this point the overall numbers in this sector must satisfy:

Siege: each sector must have a minimum of (16 minus tactics level)% melee troops initially assigned.

Assault: each sector must have a minimum of (16-2 x tactics level)% melee troops initially assigned.

Reserve troops may move one sector per phase (in 3 phases they could move to the opposite side and in 1 more join in the fight there).


Warriors assigned as cavalry will not take an active part.

Infantry carry out the actual assault. Pioneers are troops used to fill moats and ditches, and to operate battering rams, you may wish to give them armour (but they cannot use shields - nor equivalents).

Heavy weapons troops operate ballistae and catapults from outside the range of Village archers and probably don't need armour (Village catapults and ballista’s do reach heavy weapons troops).

Other items useable in sieges include ladders, assault bridges and siege towers.

11.5 Blockading

Where a Village is adjacent to an ocean, river, or lake, the sector is designated as the port sector and cannot be occupied by the besieging force unless it also contains ships willing to blockade the Village site.

If the Village site is not blockaded the defender may continue to bring ships in and send them out of the Village site even though besieged.

If the besieger uses ships to blockade then a naval battle must occur between the ships seeking entrance or exit from the Village.


If the besieged puts no vessels into the water to engage the Sieger, then any sort of Warship/Longship/Galley will be sufficient to blockade the waterfront sector. That is, in the absence of any resistance, ONE Warship/Longship/Galley could be enough to siege the waterfront sector. If there is resistance then the blockading ships will need to supply 2 warriors per yard of fortification or defeat the resistance in naval battle. The remaining 5 sectors need to have 1/5 of remaining (once crew has been deducted) Siege Troops assigned - noting Tactics limitations.

Blockading ships may come under fire from besieged catapults/ballistae as long as there are towers in the appropriate sector.

The waterfront hex is not considered to have breaches - unless Heavy Weapons fire from attacking ships create them. If Breaches are created Marines may attack through them. If a Moat or Ditch is in place, vessels may carry dirt as part of its cargo allowance (see Rules for filling Moats/Ditches).


Note: Assaults may also be conducted from vessels. The remaining 5 sectors need to have 1/5 of remaining (once crew has been deducted) Assault Troops assigned - noting Tactics limitations.

The defender may hold their ships behind walls OR put them into the water to engage the besieging ships - and each turn of the siege they may choose one or the other option. But the defending navy must defeat (that is, sink or capture all attacking “warship” vessels) of the attacking navy in order to break the siege - in the event of a draw, the siege may continue.


Catapults assigned to blockading and which are in the sectors neighbouring the port sector may have a single attempt of fire at any ships entering/exiting the Village.


11.6 DeVA


Should the Sieging or Assaulting Tribe wish to enforce this against a Village (envillaged) it should be announced at the start of the Turn "Deny extra Village Activities (DeVA)". However, extra Village Activities may be conducted by an envillaged Tribe if it announces that it is prepared to enter Field Combat against the potential besieging or assaulting Tribe in that turn (assuming it had not committed itself to remaining within its fortifications on the previous turn). A Tribe may announce "DeVA" and then move - but it must also conduct an Assault.


"Assault" MEANS that you are attacking a site (and is required if DeVA) is to be invoked.


Should you to leave open the option of attacking in the field, but not attacking if the defender retreats behind fortifications, you should Order "Attack x but do not Assault if they retreat behind Fortifications".


Note: unless under Siege, the Defender has the option of taking the Field or retreating behind fortifications. However, under these circumstances DeVA cannot be invoked.


DeVA can be enforced with long range weapon attack (for example, Ballistae). Heavy weapons troops operate ballistae and catapults from outside the range of Village archers. However, if the attack against the Village is an Assault (as opposed to Siege) any Infantry assigned still come under Village missile range. Heavy Weapons troops operating from outside Village missile range may be attacked by Infantry dispatched by the Village.

Assaults are a turn to turn proposition - whereas Sieges lock the Village in once it commits itself to its fortifications (as long as the Siege is maintained).

DeVA can be enforced with long range weapon attack (for example, Ballistae). Heavy weapons troops operate ballistae and catapults from outside the range of Village archers. However, if the attack against the Village is an Assault (as opposed to Siege) any Infantry assigned to a sector still come under Village missile range. Heavy Weapons troops operating from outside Village missile range may be attacked by Infantry dispatched by the Village.

If under Siege or DeVA a Village may not Scout, or Transfer Goods to outside Units.

Troops assigned to the Reserve during an Assault are also deemed to be outside Village missile range.

11.8 Mountains and sieges

11.9 Village Setup

11.9.1 Sectors

Villages are arranged circularly with 120 yards minimum circumference.

Each section is one sixth the circumference of the Village in length.

11.9.2 Gates

In sector 1 (unless otherwise stated) is a “gate”. Other gates, and barred gates, may be placed in other sectors of the wall, to allow sortieing. When a gate opens over a ditch or moat there is a 3 yard width filling the ditch or moat opposite it. A wooden gate = 1 damage point and a Barred gate = 2.


11.9.3 The Keep.

The Village keep is the last bastion of defence against the evil invaders. A Village under siege/assault with a keep may specify a condition under which the population will retreat to the keep. Note that as the gates of the keep must be drawn shut prior to the entry of too many besiegers, you are likely to be leaving a few martyrs outside to be butchered.

If the Village is walled, the keep may not be attacked by ballistae or catapults, but may be attacked by battering ram once the siegers are inside the external walls.

Keeps are similar to walls in construction with length and layers in stones.

The keep must have a minimum circumference of 40 yards OR (stones(in 1 layer)/40 yards) available for breaching.

Missile troops may be employed against keeps - normally the defender may utilise 1 missile troop (stone dropper/oil pourer) per yard of circumference.

11.9.4 Gates

Gates are usually the way in and out of a Village. They have 3 breaches across any ditch or moat already in place. Once a gate is destroyed it creates a 3 wide breach according to all other rules. Breaching a gate is the same as breaching a wooden wall.

Breaching a barred gate is the same as breaching a 10’ stone wall.

Opening Gates

If the defenders in a gate sector are routed, and the attacker has ladders, the gate deemed to be opened in the following phase giving the attacker use of the 3 breaches. Researching the “Lock” in Metalwork might be useful.


11.9.5 Ditches

Ditches are 8' wide and 8' deep, there are 3 ways to overcome these.

1) Ladders: Your infantry may use ladders to climb down one side and up the other (1 ladder is required for 5 infantry). Leaves them quite vulnerable to Village missiles.

2) Assault bridges: You may order the infantry to cross the ditch using assault bridges, 1 bridge per yard of a sector may be used. 10 warriors can cross each bridge (that is, 2 attackers and 8 holders of ladders per bridge). Should you fail the bridges will be destroyed by the Villages before next turn.

3) Dirt Fills: Pioneers may fill the ditch prior to an assault. Each team of 2 pioneers can use 1 wagon pulled by 2 horses/cows to move dirt to ditch per prep phase. 10 loads are needed to create a 1 yard wide fill in the ditch. 10 infantry may cross each fill. Battering rams /siege towers cross only filled ditches, they need 3 fills to cross (which infantry can't use).

11.9.6 Moats

Moats are similar to ditches, but are 10' wide, 10' deep and full of water.

1) Via assault bridges, as ditches

2) Pioneers can fill moat, 30 wagon loads of dirt are required per fill.


A Ditch remains a Ditch until fully converted to a Moat (that is, cannot have a partial Moat).


11.9.7 Palisade

These are 10' high, there are 4 ways past them:

1) Ladders: Infantry can scale walls using ladders, 4 hold each ladder whilst 1 fights atop, therefore only 20% of infantry per sector engage the enemy each turn.(1 ladder is required per 5 infantry).

2) Siege towers: You can move siege towers up to the walls. Each requires 3 fills if there is a ditch/moat. 10 warriors may be assigned each tower. If the assault is unsuccessful the towers will be towed away to stop Villages destroying them. (Villagers are like that).

3) Battering Rams: May be used to breach walls. Rams take up room like siege towers. Rams operate in prep phases: in its first prep phase a ram has 30% chance of damaging the wall; this increases by 15% for each subsequent prep phase. Each ram needs minimum 8 warriors to operate, you may wish to allow for losses.

4) Catapults: May be used to breach the wall, each prep phase a catapult fires 1 stone if you have them, at the wall, each stone has 10% chance of damaging the wall (causing 1 damage point), this can be increased by using warriors with heavy weapons skill. If you have no stones then you can still use the catapult but with less chance of damaging (1/4 the damage done by a stone).


11.9.8 Stone Wall and StoneTowers

Walls can be 10', 15', 20' high.

Breaching: two damage points are needed to penetrate a 10’ wall, four damage points for a 15’ wall (6), and 6 damage points for a 20’ wall (12). (Thus to create one complete breach through a 20’ wall requires 12 damage points.)

10’ wall = 2 Damage points (same for Palisade)

15’ wall = 4 Damage points Total = 6

20’ wall = 6 Damage points Total = 12


Towers need 50 damage points to penetrate. Towers can be targeted to destroy – a destroyed tower creates 1 breach. Thus a 120 yard site with 6 stone towers (the maximum) may have maximum of 20+1 breaches per sector. Towers also strengthen the walls to 10 yards on each side of the Tower by one damage point per wall (that is, 1 point for a 10’ wall, 2 points for a 15’ etc). So a 120 yard 10’ stone wall with 6 stone towers will enjoy 3 damage points for its entire length.


1) Ladders: may be used against 10' walls as per Palisades.

2) Siege Towers: work for all stone walls as per Palisades (Lower walls are easier).

3) Rams: may be used, but the chance of damage is 10% + 5% per phase each phase.

4) Catapults may be used, at 5% per stone, more if heavy weapons troops used.


Palisades are demolished (on a sector by sector basis) when Stone Walls are built. If you are building a stonewall to replace a Palisade the stonewall doesn't add to defence until a complete sector is finished. Thus some sites might be composed of Palisade and Stone Walls in different sectors.

The Palisade can be extended whilst a stone wall is built. When the stone wall is finally finished the Palisade is torn down for use in a celebratory bonfire. Any Wooden Towers may be transferred to the stone wall.


11.10 Methods of over coming Defenses.

Overcoming the various types of fortification require different techniques and equipment.

Note: breaches are 1 yard wide. 10 warriors can attack through a breach, 15 may defend.

A Gate in any Sector has 3 Breaches automatically. A basic Ditch or Moat has only one.

(10 can cross a fill/breach every phase and 10 can enter a breach (in a Wall) every phase thus 10 can enter a breach in phase one and another 10 join them in phase 2 and so on). Warriors may only use a breach in the phase(s) after it was created.


Equipment:

1) Ladders 4 warriors to hold 1 warrior to fight, these can only be used on wood and 10’ stone walls.

2) Battering rams: 8 warrior to wield (these warriors can fight at the breach if they create one).

3) Catapult: 6 warriors to man. fires 1 stones per prep phase.

4) Siege towers: 20 warriors to push. 10 Infantry to fight from them. (30 warriors in all.)

5) Ballistae: 4 warriors to man. Fires 1 shaft per prep phase, and attacks defenders on the walls (remains outside missile range other than the defenders heavy weapons).

11.11 Capture

Generally a Village is captured when 40% of its population are wounded in an attack.

Only 30% of an immobile Element need be wounded.

The remaining population is made slave, up to the limit of one slave per attacking Tribe member - increased by shackles. All remaining possessions of the Village are yours, but skills are lost.

You may set up your own Village on the site to take advantage of existing works.

A Village will also surrender if it suffers 40% losses from combat plus thirst, starvation or sanitation from the start of a siege. Losses are deducted as follows: Combat, Thirst, Starvation, Sanitation.




12. VILLAGE RULES


12.1 Village general

See 'Meeting House Construction' and 'Splitting The Tribe' in the main rules.

Setting up a Village can bring tremendous advantages, or at least versatility, to your clan (a lot of quarrying anyway).

12.1.1 Village-site

The Village-site is the buildings and defenses.

Villages are the non-moving counterparts of the Tribes of your clan.

More than one Village may inhabit a Village-site. (The Village-site is recorded on the page of the first Tribe to occupy it and to become a Village).

Only one clan can occupy a Village-site and use its facilities. more than one clan can build Village-sites in a hex, but they do not support each other during times of assault or siege.

Village-sites may be established on any terrain.

12.1.2 Abandoned Sites

Once a site is abandoned the locals are highly likely to take it, or destroy it. In any case, do not expect to find it again. Any exchange of site should be done in the turn. If an aggressor is in the hex and you leave, surrender etc then the site is theirs.

If you want to destroy your own site you must do so during Activities. Destroying structures is like reverse Engineering using half the people required to build.

12.1.3 Tribe to Village

When founding a Village from your Tribe you may give it any peoples/animals or items that you desire. You may also give it some of the Tribes skills, wholly or partially. (eg if Tribe had herding 10 you could give 4 levels to the Village and leave the Tribe with 6 - you could). Note that if the Village has less than 50 people it will not be able to make skill attempts of its own.

After a Village is established people may still be swapped with the Tribe, but an upper limit of 5% of a population may be transferred per month. The Village/Tribe could be assimilated completely into the other in 1 month, but all skills of the assimilated group would be lost.

12.2 Activities.

Only immobile Elements and Villages may perform the following Village tasks. For a Tribe to become a Village the Tribe must have a meeting house and declare its intention to change to the GM.

In general, metals are installed at the rate of 10lbs per person. See Stonework for some stone alternatives to metal.

12.2.2 Wood in stone buildings

At Woodwork 4 you may use logs in stone buildings. Logs may replace up to 10% of stones in towers and keeps, each log replaces 10 stones.

12.2.3 Stone buildings:

Logs may be replaced by stones at a rate of 10 stones per log at the same engineering level and with equivalent stonework level replacing woodwork where appropriate.

12.2.4 Stripping metal from buildings

The metal placed in buildings can be stripped away producing half the metal placed in the building, half the actives required to place the metal are needed to strip it away. Logs and Stones cannot be reclaimed.

12.9 Ferrying and Barges

A Ferry has 30MP (60 if 8 crew). To cross a river and return takes 1MP. Ferrying may also occur across a single hex of Ocean or Lake (costs 8 MV points to cross and return). All ferrying by Ferry requires a Jetty at each end of the leg (a Jetty may be built on the opposite side of the leg without a Meeting House).

Goods may be transferred during the Ferrying process but are effectively treated as occurring After Movement (that is, you give one transfer order for the sum total of goods transferred).

Other vessels may ferry (1 MP to row, 1.5 to sail).

A Ferry operates only on the hexside on which it was built and/or may be transferred to a unit on the opposite bank as long as a Jetty is present (and could thus require that a Village define where in the hex it is). During ferrying normal admin/dip requirements can be temporarily suspended to allow for entities being split up (1 on each side of the river, one in the middle), the ferry and crew do not constitute a separate element/fleet. Normal requirements must be met at the end of the turn and a split may be required. Skill attempts may even be shanghaied to cater (slightly retrospectively) for the need to split a stranded group in two.

Barges have 30 MP + 2 x Row skill, they may cross ford hexsides and operate above fords. Barges may only operate on rivers or lakes and during the movement phase.

Regardless of size, a ferried unit will start on one bank and end on the other.

Players should indicate the details of Ferrying in their Orders (for example, who is doing it and with what vessels) – including the calculation of weights/carrying capacities.

12.17 Fair

The Fair is held twice per Year (the First Month of Summer, and the First Month of Winter) during which Triball may be played as a Cultural event and Trade will be conducted using a full Price List (see below).


Requirement

1. Tribes may Trade and/or play Triball - they may, but are not required to, do both.

2. To Trade requires a Trading Post if a Village or Eco5 if nomadic. An Element may conduct the Fair but it must satisfy these requirements. Only one Tribe per Clan may engage in the Fair.

3. Only limited Activities are permitted during the Fair - these are Hunting, Herding, Apiarism, Cooking and Farming (and any Defence/Security/Suppression and/or Religious obligations) but Scouting is not permitted. Triball

a. Every Clan (at 1 Tribe per Clan) will be paid in Silver for their participation in Triball. Payment will depend on Skill level, number of warriors, horses and clubs involved. A maximum of 500 Warriors, Horses and Clubs are permitted to participate and these will not be available should combat occur.

b. Triball generates (Number of participants) times (2 + Triball/2 +Eco/4) Silver. A horse counts as 1 participant and a club as 0.5. (500 Warriors, 500 Horses and 500 Clubs = 1250 participants).

c. Playing Triball counts against the 10-item trade limit.

Trade

a. The Tribe trading at the Fair may Buy and/or Sell a total of 10 items. For example, Selling 5 items and Buying 5 items constitutes 10 items.

b. You may trade at ONE location only (but you can choose which).

c. Art, Music, Dance and Cooking may be “sold” as part of the Trade activity - but participation in each Discipline counts against the 10 item trade limit.

Each of these Cultural activities generates (number of participants) times (2 + Skill level/4 +Eco/4) Silver.

Or {Number of Participants x (8+Skill+Eco)}/4

A maximum of 500 may participate in each activity.

Note: musical instruments count as half a participant for selling music. You are limited to 1 instrument per musician.

Note: players are responsible for calculating the Silver generated from Cultural Activities.

d. Trading is deemed to occur at the start of the Turn.

12.18 Joint Projects (Engineering and Shipbuilding)

12.18.1 Intra Clan

For any projects within a Clan, the requisite skill levels MUST be owned by one of the participating Tribe/Villages.

However labor from another portion of the clan, and skills of this clan may be utilised under the following conditions:-

Total the skill levels required for the project, eg a shrine requires Eng5 Wood1 Stone2 = 8 skill levels.

Underskilled labor is reduced in effectiveness by being multiplied by 10/(10 + number of skills in deficit).

For example, in the case of a shrine being built by two Villages when one of the Villages has the necessary skills, and where the other has Wood3, but no Eng or Stonework (and so is 7 skills short of the mark).

Any labor supplied by this Village would be multiplied by 10/(10+7).

Therefore if 170 labourers were supplied by the Village they would only count as 100 towards the shrine.


This procedure can also be used when neither portion of the clan possesses the required skills INDIVIDUALLY.

In the above example, assume that one Village had Wood and Stonework sufficient for the shrine (but no Eng), the other Village sufficient Eng but no other relevant skills.

Village labor would be 3 skill levels short (10/13 or approx. 0.77) on the one hand, and 5 skills short (10/15 or 0.67) on the other.

Note: 20% of labor required must be provided by any Village/Tribe supplying the skill/s.

Joint projects must be kept within a Clan. They cannot be conducted on researched items.

Players are responsible for calculating figures!


12.18.2 Inter Clan

Tribes from different Clans may not combine to work on projects. However, a Tribe may do Engineering for a different Clan and then transfer buildings/works across (under Transfers). If this involves access to a site then the recipient of the works must indicate that they are allowing such access. Once a Tribe has access to a site then the defensive works of that site do not come into effect should an attack be announced. Any such attack would be resolved under normal Field Combat.

13.9 Location of Villages

There are a number of locations where it is important for the purposes of naval movement to know where in a hex a Village-site is located.

This is important when the Village-site is adjacent to a ford or when situated in a hex with more than one coast (NOT more than one coast hexside, but with two separate coasts split by land). This tends to occur on narrow peninsulas and small islands.


14. The Village Under Siege


For siege purposes your Village-site perimeter is deemed to consist of 6 sectors of at least 20 yards each. Troops must be assigned to sections or sortieing parties OR to the reserve.

Warriors may be assigned to infantry, archers or heavy weapons (if your Village-site has towers) to defend the Village-site.

It should be noted that archers assigned a second weapon will make more effective use of it than battlefield archers, due to the obvious distinctions between assaults and preparation and the prospect of imminent slavery.

14.1 Seeking refuge

When attacked all people and livestock are brought inside the Village-site defenses.

Each person, horse and cow takes up 5 square yards of space, goats take 1 square yard, Elephants take 10. Excess animals are left outside (specify which type of animal you regard as excess).

When all warriors in a section are wounded, the enemy starts butchering your Villagers, when 40% of your population is wounded your Village capitulates. Note that morale and whether there are any religious structures in the Village can modify this figure.

14.2 Heavy weapons

Heavy weapons may be utilised from towers (see tower construction).

Ballistae are anti-personnel devices to be used against archers and heavy weapon troops (Prep), and also advancing infantry (but not pioneers).

Catapults are anti-device weapons, they fire at enemy catapults/ballistae/siege towers/bridges. Should no such equipment exist in the sector that the catapult covers, it is capable of reaching adjacent attacking sectors.

If the wall is cleared of defenders, ballistae/catapults are destroyed.

Heavy Weapons cannot be moved from Sector to Sector during an Assault/Siege.

14.3 Non missile defence

Non-missile Village defenders may drop 1 stone each/round on pioneers operating rams and once more when infantry begin scaling the walls (no effect on infantry entering via a breach). Alternately 2 defenders without missile weapons may pour 50 boiling wax or oil (if available) on a similar range of clients using a cauldron.

14.4 Sector identification

The 6 Village sectors are standardised as follows: Sector 1 will be the north sector, Sector 2 NE, Sector 3 SE, 4 S, 5 SW and 6 NW.

Where a Village-site has adjacent ocean, lake or river hexsides, the defenses in the sectors corresponding to the water will have greater protection. The attacker may not deploy in these sectors unless on board a ship.

Where a Village is located in a mountain hex with adjacent pass hexsides, similar restrictions relate to sectors which do NOT contain passes. Wagons, catapults and ballistae may only be allocated to the sector relating to the direction of the pass by which they entered, unless constructed within the hex, in which case they must be built in a specific sector and can only be moved from their location if either the siege/assault is successful or they have been built in a sector which contains a pass in which case they can be moved out with the Tribe if it leaves via that sectors pass.

14.5 Reserve

The reserve is a force of warriors kept back to be utilised in sectors where losses/breaches have occurred.

For every level of tactics you possess, 10% of your troops assigned to infantry or cavalry may be allocated to the reserve.

The conditions of release are up to the player-it will take one phase for the reserve to take up position. The reserve may be used in as many areas as you like.



14.6 Troop movements

Village defenders may be conditionally moved during siege phases (after phase 1).

The 6 Village sectors are arranged consecutively in a circle 1-6, sector 1 is adjacent to 6. The reserve is central and adjacent to all sectors.

It takes troops 1 phase to move to an adjacent sector, during which time they are not available for combat. Movement of troops is bound to be based upon the situation in which you find yourself so consider possibilities carefully when stipulating conditional troop movements.

For example, Phase 1 orders might be “If no missile troops assigned to this sector, move 5 archers to sector 3 if it is under attack by missile troops otherwise to reserve and thence sector 5” (if they did move to sector 5 then they would take no part in combat in phases 1 and 2)

NB that troops passing through the reserve will count against the limit allowed.

Troops utilising the reserve AT ANY TIME during a siege may still not exceed reserve limits however troops may be moved to adjacent sectors (including to/from reserve if available).

14.7 Sortieing vs Siege

1.Defenders in a Sector may Sortie vs Siegers in the same Sector if there is a gate in the sector. (Note that if you install gates you also install breaches in front of them if there is a moat and/or ditch). Gates cannot be built once the Siege is in place, Walls cannot be pulled down.

If the site is unwalled then the sector(s) through which the sortieing parties engage must be supplied (each) with 3 Assault Bridges. Assault Bridges may be built during Activities phase while under Siege.


2.This results in all sortiers dispatched vs all siegers (any HvyW troops assigned may use a melee weapon) - in the sector in a single phase Infantry field combat. In effect this will involve up to 1/5 or 1/6th warriors on defence per sector depending on whether the Village has a waterfront.

Sortiers will be deemed to be in “Attack” mode, whereas Siegers will be in “Defend” mode. The Siegers are +0.04 to Morale. The Reserve plays no role in this.


3.This may be done on any turn the Village is under siege apart from the first, that is, the turn in which the Siege is announced.


4.No looting takes place - however if the besieging forces in the sector(s) rout then any Heavy Weapons deployed in that sector are destroyed.


5.Any losses of Village warriors are counted against the 40% surrender rule. Casualties to the Attacker are deducted from siege/assault numbers in a 1/5th or 1/6th ratio.


6.Sortiers are not available for the Village’s Defence (this might be relevant in a situation where one Clan has a Village under Siege while another Assaults it).


7.If the Sieger’s troops fall to below 2 per yard, the Siege is broken. (This would be the main point of Sortieing).


8.Sorties are possible against Tribes enforcing DeVA. However, the number of sortiers sent out if not limited by sector/gates etc and they will engage all DeVA warriors. Should the attacking force be routed in the single phase infantry melee then the Village may perform extra Village Activities on the following turn.


14.8 Siege life

During a siege a Village may only perform manufacturing and Village activities and may not conduct extra-Village Activities such as Hunting and Mining. Fishing is possible if it has a waterfront without unfriendly vessels occupying it.

Walls etc may only be repaired, not extended. Siege activities phases are those in the activity phase following a siege attack (which is during the event encounter phase - even if the siege is broken off later in that turn). Villages will require water during a siege (see wells) and animals will require fodder and water but won't need to be herded.

15.3 Getting lost

Ships blown off course in gales (or even in strong winds if Nav/Sea skills aren't high and/or the ship is operating outside ideal conditions).

If this happens you will not be told how far the ships travelled or the path they took to get there (although wind direction will give you a good idea!). You will be informed if you sighted land.


15.4 Damage

The greater proportion of hull/sail damage will reduce MP accordingly.

Damage can occur to either the hull (body) of a vessel or to the rigging (cloth and rope). It can be caused by fighting, bad weather or just general wear and tear. Minor damage is recorded as tenths of a damage point, eg 0.5 damage points means that 5 points of minor damage have been sustained.

If 10 points of minor damage have been suffered then it is deemed that major damage (whole number damage) has occurred.

Rigging damage can be repaired either at land or sea (it is more difficult at sea) but only minor hull damage may be repaired at sea. Furthermore, once hull damage equals half the DR of the ship it may only be reduced below half DR in a shipyard.

Damage to rigging or hull will reduce movement proportionally, if both are damaged then the lower movement will apply. Damage to rigging will not, however, affect rowing movement.

15.4.1 Marine hazards

Certain ocean hexes/hexsides will contain dangerous marine hazards such as rocks or reefs.

These can cause varying degrees of damage, even sinking a vessel. Appropriate naval skills will reduce the chance of damage occurring.

15.4.2 Wear and tear

Lack of maintenance generally results in .1 damage/month to both hull and rigging. Even on land!

Just being at sea usually results in a further .1 hull damage. When out of sight of land (unless in calm waters like a lake, sheltered bay or inland sea), caravels will damage more readily (.2/month).

In addition adverse weather conditions (strong winds/gales) are also likely to damage ships (especially if seamanship is low, with caravels more prone than clinkers to take damage).

15.4.3 Sinking

Ships may be damaged/destroyed outright by adverse weather, especially when naval skills are low and when operating in less than ideal conditions.

Any ship taking its full DR in damage will automatically sink.

15.5 Beaching and Launching

Coastal (land) hexes are also considered to include some water, ships may (and in fact only do) travel through coast hexes. If a player wishes to actually beach in a hex it WONT cost any MP's (you may wish to beach in order to fill up with water or perform land based activities). Launching costs for beached ships are Boat, Barge 1 MP, Fisher/coaster/longship 3 MP. Galleys/merchant/warship 5 MP.

Note that a trader CANNOT beach, it may only berth at a jetty. Berthing and launching at a jetty costs NO MP.

15.6 Damage repair

(see damage in Move/combat) covered by maintain boat skill

Note damage cannot be repaired at sea during a gale.

15.6.1 Repair rigging damage

On land Minor damage can be repaired at a rate of .1 per person (NB you may repair all minor damage by treating it as a 1 point of major damage)

Major damage can be repaired at a rate of 2 people, 1 cloth and 1 rope per point.

At sea TWICE the labour is required.

15.6.2 Repair hull Damage

On land Minor damage may be repaired at a rate of 2 people per 0.1 (all minor damage can be treated as 1 point of major damage if desired)

Major damage requires 10 people and 2 logs per point.

At sea Minor damage can be repaired at a rate of 5 people per .1.

Major damage CANNOT be repaired at sea.


If Hull damage = HALF the ships damage rating (DR) or more, damage may only be reduced to half DR EXCEPT in a shipyard.

15.7 Maintenance

(10 people/lvl till 10) (see damage)

On land 1 person can maintain 10 DR (Hull & Rigging) of ships, this is double if vessels are in a boatshed.

At sea 1 person (or 2 crew if half movement) can maintain 5 DR (hull & rigging combined). For example, a coaster (4DR hull+ 4DR rigging) requires 2 people to maintain.

17. Naval Combat


17.1 Engagement

A Fleet may engage in Naval Combat under the following circumstances.

1.It has an Order to Engage AND

2.It occupies the same hex (or hexside in the case of a River) as another Fleet at the start or the end of Movement

OR

3.It is stationary (Patrolling) and another Fleet passes through its hex (or hexside in the case of a River) or any of the six surrounding hexes.


17.1.1 Phases

There are 3 phases in Naval Combat.


1.The Fleet (as a whole) will attempt to out Manoeuvre the other Fleet. The successful Fleet is deemed then to have its individual ships out Manoeuvre its opposition. The player should announce their intentions to Engage or Flee should their Fleet be successful - the Winning MF is recognised with respect to player conditional Orders..


2.At this point individual ships may Manoeuvre to HeavyWeapons/Ram/Missile Fire/Board. Heavy Weapon fire is conducted outside the range of other missiles.


Note: the elevation of Warships gives them 1.2 for Archery and HvyW.

Note: Ballistae damage will cause 1/2 damage to crew and 1/2 to Vessels. (1 Casualty Point = 1 point of hull damage)

Catapults cause full damage to Vessels (as Ballistae, 1 Casualty Point = 1 point of Hull damage)

The opposing (losing) Fleet may attempt to Ram at 50% reduction to RF, missile fire may be exchanged at -4 to Arc etc, but a Board may not be attempted.

3.Continue 2 or Board. A Board results in Melee - 75% of available Marines may Board (increased with Research, for example, Grappling Hooks).

If a boarding takes place a Combat may ensue. This would act as a single 1 Sector Battle with Mariner skill replacing Combat. When firing missiles from one ship to another it is Arc/HvyW skill that is used.

Note: Vessels will engage each other 1:1. Where one Fleet outnumbers the other, then 2:1 determined randomly if not Ordered by players.


17.1.2 Damage

Ships surviving a Battle will be assessed for Damage. Damage is assessed vs Fleets as a whole (not individual ships).

Where possible, crews will always be maintained at their maximum for the individual ships in as Fleet. That is, Warriors will be assigned to crew duties before there may be assigned to Warrior duties. The only exception to this is if a Fleet decides to remain stationary for the turn.

Naval damage does not decrease Movement rate - but may allow the opposing Fleet to pursue and engage on the following turn. The Pursuit Factor (PF) is decisive here.


17.1.3 Order of Events

1.Calculate total MF’s.

2.Resolve Manoeuvres (phase 1)

3.Calculate damage for Rams, Missile fire etc (ships are 1:1 or 2;1 etc if outnumber) Ships sink if HD is > 100%. If a ship sinks 1/2 people are lost, 1./4 are rescued and 1/4 taken as slave. (Phase 2)

4.Recalculate MF’s after any sunken ships.

5.Resolve phase 3 and calculate MF’s

6.Finally calculate Pursuit Factor (PF), if (Nav + Sea + Capt + TP+d12)/3 x Morale x Hull Damage(Winner) exceeds (Nav + Sea + Capt + TP+d12)/3 x Morale x Hull Damage(Loser) then Winning Fleet by more than 2 it may pursue as long as it does not contain a slower ship (for example, a Fleet with a Trader A may not pursue a Fleet with a Boat B as its slowest ship).

Note: A sunken ship counts as losing half its Hull rating for the purposes of Hull Damage calculation.(equivalent to losing Morale). As does a ship that is Boarded and which loses Combat.

This means that a single mobile craft will probably not out run a larger Fleet.


Naval Combat Orders

If a player is invited to submit Orders for Naval Combat they should indicate People and Goods for each vessel individually.


17.1.4 Surrender

A Fleet will surrender if:

1.It suffers >50% Hull Damage,

2.Is caught and its Warriors are outnumbered 3:1 or worse and it has an inferior MF.


17.2 Patrolling

Ships can be given a patrol order, they will then patrol the hex on a lookout for other fleets, conditional orders will then be applied to determine the actions of the patrolling fleet. If a moving fleet enters a patrolled hex the patrolling fleet may intercept.

17.3 Factors influencing combat

The skills of combat, archery, heavy weapons will be limited by your mariner skill, if the mariner skill is lower than the combat skill it is used instead.

At sea instead of leadership, captaincy will determine how well led your troops are.

Seamanship and navigation will determine how manoeuvres are carried out.

17.4 Manoeuvres possible

Each ship taking part in the combat must manoeuvre with one of the 5 possible manoeuvres.

17.4.1 Manoeuvre to defend

Your ship will try to keep its distance from the opposing fleets, no offensive activity will take place.

17.4.2 Manoeuvre to withdraw

Your ship will try to flee the battle,. a combination of captaincy, navigation, seamanship, ship type, wind direction, effective speeds and relative positioning will be the main determinants.

17.4.3 Manoeuvre to missile fire

Your ship will attempt to keep its distance while attacking the opponents ship with any warriors and equipment assigned to missile fire.

17.4.4 Manoeuvre to ram

Your ship will attempt to close with a enemy ship and ram it.

17.4.5 Manoeuvre to board

Your ship will attempt to close with a enemy ship so your marines can board and capture it.

17.5 Defence points

Each ship has a defence point rating which is the number of warriors that can be effectively employed in defensive and offensive action during missile and boarding manoeuvres.

Defence points is basically free deck space and any fortifications space the ship has available.

Anyone rowing may not participate in a melee on a ship.

17.6 Using Ships for Defence

If at the end of Movement a Tribe falls under attack in a hex in which it has access to its own ships (Ocean, Lake, River) it may opt to board those ships (in the same way that it may opt to fall behind fortifications should they be present). They may not move out of the hex as such but are deemed to be out of the way of combat. Should the whole Tribe be able to board ships and if the attacker has none then it is able to avoid combat. If attacking and defending Tribes are already together in the hex (that is, at the start of the Turn) this option is not available.


Conditions

1.If the whole Tribe cannot fit its entire people on board those that cannot fit will be left behind. However, under these conditions it must have sufficient Adm levels to support what is essentially an extra Element.

2.If all Goods cannot be taken aboard the player needs to specify what will be left behind - or else the GM will.

3.If on an Ocean then fresh water needs to be carried aboard.

4.The Tribe is deemed to end the turn at Sea, Lake, River etc and will not be able to do land based Activities on the following turn.

5.On the following Turn it should Order, Movement, Land or Hold.



18. Fishing and Whaling


18.1 Fishing

Fleets can perform fishing from a Village, coastal or river. Launch, fish beach instructions apply.

Fleets can also fish off the coast, and in the deep blue.

Hexes may contain fishing grounds that have a extra abundance of fish, and rivers may have salmon runs.

When doing Fishing please indicate if you are Fishing in a Coastal (Land) hex or out at Sea (or Lake) – that is, away from land.

18.1.1 Village fishing

Village fishing involves fishing during activities phase by vessels based in a Village or by fishing elements/Tribes beached in a land hex.

18.1.2 Naval fishing

Firstly 'line' fishing may be performed normally (in the activities phase) by people assigned to the fishing activity.

Net fishing is performed ONLY during the portion of the turn NOT devoted to movement. That is, the best possible catch will generally be made when no movement is performed (other factors being equal).

Note that when the ship is NOT moving for a portion of a turn, crew members become available for partial activities.

For example, a vessel with a crew of 6 which only used half movement could have these people assigned to net fishing or could assign 6 halves (ie 3) to maintenance, repair, salting, line fishing, etc.

Net fishing can occur before, during or after movement. eg you could order a ship to fish for 10 movement points then move, or to move, fish for a certain number of MP then move again.

Note that vessels have an “Effective Fisher Limit” people above this number (and 'partial' people count fully for this purpose) will not contribute much.

Fishing may not be performed from Canoe or Kayak.

19. Religion


Formal religions may be established within the campaign. This does not preclude in any manner a Tribe or clan from having their own 'unofficial' set of beliefs that they pursue, but for game purposes these are the guidelines.

19.1 Establishment

Any TWO CLANS stating that they wish to establish one may establish a religion. These Clans MUST have at least ONE of their Tribes or Villages taking Religion as their PRIMARY skill attempt.

Other than when establishing a religion a Tribe/Village MAY NOT attempt the religion skill without already being a member of a religion. If the religion skill is attempted (including where a Tribe is intending to establish a religion but the other player does not also state in their orders that they wish to help establish one, the skill attempt is deemed to fail automatically).

Clans as a whole join a religion, you cannot have part of your clan join a religion and another part remain unaffiliated or joins another religion (without research - such research would also mean that if these diversifications were achieved, that people transfers would not be possible between portions of the clan not being of the same religious inclination)

19.2 Benefits

Formal religions offer (initially) TWO benefits, a Primary & a Secondary.

These benefits take the form of an increase in productivity in areas such as Herding (general or specific), Hunting, Mining, Fishing, Farming, or in Population Increase, or Battle Prowess, or Scouting (general or specific missions or survival) etc.

Set output levels for manufacture, movement rates etc are NOT good areas for benefits as the amount of benefit will remain unknown.

The degree of benefit for a Tribe/Village will be determined by:-

1. The severity of the restrictions/festivals placed upon the members

2. The Tribe/Village's religion skill

3. The number of clans involved in the religion

19.3 Restrictions

The only REQUIRED restriction is that AT LEAST ONE MONTH of the year must be designated as a FESTIVAL/RELIGIOUS month.

A minimum proportion of ACTIVE POPULATION must be stipulated for taking part in festivities. The festivities may actually be performed by warriors/active’s (inactives are automatically presumed to be taking part) but the proportion is expressed in terms of active population (ie you could have 20% or 120% which would require warrior participation definitely).

People taking part in festivities/prayer/meditation are not available for other activities (so don't forget Herder’s).

The more inconvenient the festival the more benefits it generates (that is, the more people involved the better, also winter festivals, whilst tempting are not as rewarding as those set in autumn, which are not as rewarding as summer, which in turn is not as rewarding as spring.) You may wish to have more than one festival (eg you may wish to have a mid winter and mid summer festival, or you might have an autumn festival involving most of the Tribe and a smaller festival, in, the first month of spring requiring only a small number of Tribe members).

You may also make other restrictions for your religion, which may or may not apply permanently or just during festivals.

For example, you may require some form of sacrifice (animal, human-slaves/prisoners or some item eg burning vast amounts of fodder or logs).

It may be required that automatic attack (non truced Tribes) orders exist during festivities.

The eating of particular animals flesh may be forbidden (horses, say or cannibalism).

Wine or other alcoholic concoctions may have to be consumed at festivals.

Certain skills may be forbidden.

It is up to the religions. The more “repressive” a religion, the more benefit.

Note: Fasting (a requirement for some religious groups) is considered to happen over three or four days and doesn't eliminate the need to eat provs (over the month).


19.4 Observance

It is up to members of a religion to remember to observe the religious festivities. Failure to observe a festival/restriction will result in morale losses and benefit losses for the at fault Tribe (at the GM’s discretion), other members of the religion will also be penalised by NOT counting the forgetful Tribe as a religion member when assessing their bonuses.



19.5 Varying a religion

It is possible to change a religion’s details, however it isn’t easy.

Proposed changes must be submitted to the GM, these will be issued to all members of the religion to vote upon. A majority vote is required to change details.

The only method of abstaining is for you to default for the turn the vote takes place, if no vote is indicated then it will be recorded as a NO vote.

If changes are successful, any minorities opposing the changes will have the option of either leaving the religion without penalty (other than loss of religion skill levels) or remaining true to their faith in a religion with the original details EVEN IF they would be the only clan in that religion (that is, a Schism).

For example, two schisming Clans will have a membership of two in the original Religion. The remaining majority starts neo-Religion with 2 less members but with all other benefits retained. The effective result is loss of membership - but this is likely to be great for one of the groups.


If the proposals are unsuccessful the proposer loses morale, if they wish to leave the religion they must do so in the normal fashion, losing skill levels etc.

19.6 Leaving a Religion

This is easy to do, just say so, HOWEVER the repercussions can be severe, depending on how much religion skill you have. All levels of religion skill will be lost.

AT LEAST one morale point (.01) will be lost per level of skill.

There is a chance of Tribe members who adhere to the religion leaving, possibly taking some skills with them. The extent of the damage will depend on how involved with the religion you were. If leaving a religion the entire clan must leave (barring research).


Religions are fascinating things, they are most powerful when they have a significant membership, high skill levels are obtained and restrictions are significant.

It is a good idea to make a religion fairly consistent, for example, if your religion requires that you attack non-truced Tribes (or unbelievers, maybe you won't be allowed to truce with non believers) during holy months, then it may be a good idea to opt for increased combat effectiveness as one benefit.

You may research additional benefits when religion skill 10 is attained; these topics are not the easiest as they flow through to all members of the religion (unless you are of a selfish nature).

When benefits are assessed the likely usage is an important factor, eg herding and hunting are going to be performed almost every turn, as benefits would be received every turn they wouldn't be as proportionally high as for mining increases, combat increases, healing increases.

I would not recommend that clans get involved in religions too early in their lives. Until you can cope with the demands of the restrictions (say a months hunting in summer....) the benefits are likely to be outweighed by the inconvenience.

Note that the term “religion” has been used throughout; religion skill also encompasses philosophies which may not necessarily be of a precisely religious nature (you might have an atheistic philosophy which in game terms would be treated as a religion – however, it would be targeted to undermine an existing, specific Religion ).

20. Research


Once you have attained skill level 10 in a skill you may conduct research into further application(s) of that skill. Each Tribe may research 1 skill/turn (in addition to your skill attempt). If successful you will be informed and have the option of developing that idea or scrapping it. To develop an idea you must research it; development requires that you reach a certain research level with your concept (similar but not the same as a skill check to improve skill level). Development may be suspended without losing the idea and without sacrificing your research level once half the required level is attained. Examples of research include new weapons or armour types, new animals to breed (like sheep, camels, or dogs) or improved rates of output. When researching you may suggest a specific topic (but don't need to) however, you should limit your suggestions to what is reasonable within the parameters of the game.


In general I would prefer players to come up with what they think are the appropriate considerations/details re research topics. I will then look at the proposal and approve or modify or make further suggestions etc.

If you research something that gives you an advantage over other Tribes this advantage might be undone by counter-research.


Research Lists

There will be two Lists of Research Topics - one will be publicly available (and at this stage will be comprised of topics from the “old” continents with a handful from Mangalia. The other list (Private List) will be available in entirety only to the GM.


As long as other requirements are met, a Tribe may research topics from the Public List. In addition to topics on the Public List, each Clan will be able to nominate one new topic once any Tribe in the Clan has reached Level 8 in the relevant skill. The Tribe may opt to put this topic onto the Public List (it will only appear on the list once it has been completed by the original researcher) or onto the Private List. However, 50% of a Clan’s research topics must be on the Public List.


Each 50 turns that a Clan survives enables it to nominate another new topic (regardless of whether or not it has a Level 8). The Tribe may opt to put this topic onto the Public List (once it has been completed by the original researcher) or onto the Private List. (So in practice, some of the older Clans can have at least two “secret” research topics going on at this point in time).


The rights to nominate topics either via the Lvl8 or the 50 turn requirement may be sold or traded for Silver or Gold via Auctions.

Note also that Espionage (under either Spying or Research research) is a research topic I am prepared to accept. One implication of this is that Tribes with topics on the Private List might have them more widely known.


The Public List is available upon request. However, some topics will appear in name only.

21. Politics


Politics relates to the control of a territory and is usually backed up with a military presence. For nomadic Tribes, or recently establish Villages, effective Politics is not an easy thing to come to terms with. The basis of any Political achievement occurs at Politics 10 when the basic concept of a State with a National rather than ethnic identity is hammered into the essentially xenophobic Clan populace (and the immediate locals). Once Pol10 is achieved (GL0), a claim to City-Statehood (whereupon the Chief may research Daimyo), with attendant territorial claims upon the hex occupied, is conferred upon the Village. The immediate entitlement is a right to tax the native population (in return for peace keeping and defensive commitments from the City-State). The month for collecting Taxes and the recruitment of locals must be specified by the player at the time of achieving Pol10 - and this must be kept consistent (that is, it always occurs in the same month).

In order to convince the natives of your merits, Pacifiers must be assigned to the hex (these are assigned per hex controlled and are assumed to operate their own Pacification/Scouting missions into controlled hexes surrounding the central hex of control). One per cent of warriors (of the Pol10 Tribe) need to be assigned per controlled hex - they must have mounts and full metal armour (including either Breast or Cuirass - but do not require any armour that needs research) and at least one metal weapon.

They are available for Combat duty if the combat occurs in a controlled hex. These troops are also considered to train locals in Combat etc to join the main Tribe should they be attacked (see Benefits below for more details). Politics research may modify these figures.

Control of hexes beyond the central hex is blocked by rivers, high mnts and snow mnts unless a ford or pass exists. But may extend over Oceans and Lakes though these are not counted for the purposes of Taxation or Recruit bonuses.

If a Clan takes up Nation or Statehood it must be the only Clan with a Village in the hex (that is, you cannot claim Statehood if there are alien Villages in the hex). Similarly, control cannot be extended into or through a hex in which an alien Clan already has a Village.

A foreign Tribe may be based in a controlled hex without a treaty but it cannot set up a Village and do Village things.


Extending the State:

The centre of your State is your Pol10 Village (City), and to extend the boundaries of the State you must research Governing Levels (GL's). A Pol10 City must number at least 6000 people. If the population falls below 6000 the Pol10 and GL0 will be retained but City benefits (including and researched GL’s) will be lost.

Each GL adds a 1 hex radius to the bounds of your state (which is effectively limited to a fine weather mounted scouting range - road research may extend this range).

A Clan may have more than one Pol10 City.

Note: Books may not be written for GL’s (that is, GL development must be researched independently by each Pol10 City).


Governing:

For each level of Governing, 1% of actives (from the Pol10 Tribe) must be assigned to Governing at GL0 this is zero). Building a Courthouse (Eng7, Wood3, Stn4, 12,000 stones, 500 logs) will halve the requirements.


Losing Production

If Pacifiers are not assigned, and/or Governors not appointed, control of the hex(es) is deemed to be lost for the turn and they will be unproductive for that turn. They will remain unproductive as long as they are not administered, but will return to full production when Pacifiers and Governors are reassigned. A City under Siege or DeVA will not be able to administer to controlled hexes.


Overlapping States:

Where States overlap, the hexes involved are considered disputed territories (except where the overlap involves Tribes of the same Clan). Within these hexes, and REGARDLESS OF TRUCES, states of War are deemed to exist. In disputed territories the natives will consider such dispute good enough reason to avoid paying taxation and may look unkindly on Pacifiers. In short, any political bonuses derived from these hexes will not apply.


If the hex(es) controlled by the GL of a Politics 10 Village are occupied by another (unTruced) Clan (of at least 1000 Warriors) for at least two consecutive turns, the owner of the Politics 10 village is informed that they have lost control of the said hex(es). Note, the availability of the Militia is not affected.


Treaties:

(Research; DL= sq. Root[number of disputed hexes - rounded up]). Treaties formally define control of disputed territories; each treaty must be researched separately. Either party can develop treaties. The terms must be finalised within 2 turns of development. The treaty will become operational following the turn in which both parties accept the terms of the treaty (expending 2 parchment x DL each). If any party formally rejects the treaty at any stage after the terms are finalised, it is destroyed. Treaties need not address all territories in dispute, but if it does, this aim needs to be specified at the time of researching. The terms of a treaty may not be extended to encompass territories either not in dispute or omitted at the time the research was successful.


Breaking Treaties:

Terms may be renounced in several ways. One party can research a new Treaty and, if agreed between the parties, this can replace the old one. One party could leave the area (or the game) abandoning their State. Otherwise, Research could be performed to declare an existing Treaty invalid with respect to one or more Territories, which would then return them to disputed status.


Nations (research prerequisite: Daimyo):

At Pol10, and with either Dip10 or Adm10, Clans may develop into Nations with Politics research. [Politics research; DL = 3 + numbers of Clans involved]. Nations can only be formed from States having zones of overlap between them, and Treaties governing all territories in such zones. Nations are developed in a similar fashion to Treaties. Once having been formed, dispute settlement procedures may be put in place (that is, terms may be formalised with respect to appointing an authority, allowing votes based on territories held etc). Once a Nation has been established, new Territories will become the possession of the first member whose influence reaches it. (If this should occur simultaneously, Civil War will be initiated and all Territories will be temporarily suspended. A new Treaty will need to be negotiated to deal with the territory newly in dispute as well as any other territories not previously subject to treaty. Once this is resolved the Nation is automatically re-established.


The Emperor

Nations are composed of States headed by a Daimyo. A Nation may have but one Emperor.


Adding New Members:

A prospective new member must have a zone of overlap with the State of a current member and a relation of Treaty governing all the territories in that zone. The new Nation must be researched by a current member and be accepted by all current members and the prospective member. Nations may be merged by this method (only requires 1 overlap and 1 Treaty).

There are also other signs that all is not what it seems to be. Here are some of them:

1.Running a dummy/address “player” behind the Clan - often a Web based email account.

2.Not providing a home postal address.

3.Sending correspondence/Orders from different “players” from the one Account/Computer.

4.Falling behind in payments to the GM.

5.Engaging in little or no personal correspondence with other players.

6.Regularly being the subject of complaint by other players.

7.Never being home when someone knocks on the door to pay an “in person” visit.


None of these on their own is particularly significant - but when the factors combine I have cause for concern, and though no player has yet been evicted from the game by me this is always a possibility.


Censorship is something I do not like being called upon to administer. There have been no major problems here either, but we should always be sensitive to the possibility (especially in view of the fact that younger people do play the game).


The bottom line in all the above is that I rely largely on the integrity of the players to construct the ethical standards in the game.



26. A Final Word


The GM is sometimes called upon to interpret, clarify or expand upon the rules. This is because the rules as written cannot cover every game contingency - if they could, Tribe Net would not be open-ended. It seems to me that where there is a conflict between a rule (or a particular interpretation of that rule) and the overall interests of the game, a consideration in favour of the latter should weigh more heavily. This might at times mean relegating the interests of individual players. It goes without saying that when such decisions are called for the GM strives for impartiality and objectivity. Having said this, any changes or adjustments to the rules are infrequent and usually quite minor. They are generally published in Tribe News.
